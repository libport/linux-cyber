# Chapter 7: Business and Organizational Intelligence
## Purpose
Organisational intelligence applies open source research to organisations and the people, assets, relationships and systems that make them work. Analysts use it to examine companies, non-profits, charities, political groups, governments, partnerships, cooperatives and education institutions. The discipline helps reveal ownership, influence, procurement activity, public exposure, misconduct indicators and technical infrastructure.

The Wirecard collapse shows why this work matters. Investigators combined company records, reporting, whistleblower material and open source techniques to expose fraud involving false customers, opaque entities and missing funds. Similar methods support due diligence, anti-money laundering, counter-proliferation, fraud investigation, competitive intelligence, journalism and security assessment.

Organisational intelligence also has a defensive value. Adversaries collect the same public information before phishing staff, abusing vendors, targeting facilities or identifying weak technical controls. The 2013 Target breach showed how a third-party supplier can become an entry point. The 2022 Mailchimp incident showed how employee targeting and social engineering can expose customer data and API keys. Strong analysis therefore considers both legitimate investigative value and adversarial misuse.
## Core research model
Effective organisational analysis starts with a clear research question. The question may ask whether a merger target presents risk, whether data has leaked, whether a company hides fraud, or whether a supplier creates national security exposure. Analysts then collect only the data needed to answer the question and widen the search when the evidence supports it.

High-value selectors include:
- Legal names and former names
- Incorporation dates and jurisdictions
- Parent companies, subsidiaries and holding companies
- Directors, officers, beneficial owners and shareholders
- Registered agents, addresses, phone numbers and email domains
- Contracts, awards, grants and procurement notices
- Annual reports, periodic filings and financial statements
- Lawsuits, regulatory actions, sanctions and watch lists
- Partners, suppliers, customers and university links
- Domains, IP addresses, autonomous system numbers and hosting providers

Analysts should prefer primary sources, then corroborate them with reliable secondary sources. Government registries, securities filings, court records, contract databases and sanctions lists usually carry more weight than marketing material, media reports or social media posts. Public disclosures remain useful because organisations often publish small details that gain meaning when combined with other evidence.
## Analytical safeguards
Organisational intelligence depends on careful attribution. Analysts should separate legal entities from trading names, brands, branches and websites. They should record each jurisdiction because the same name can appear in many registries and may refer to unrelated organisations. Dates also matter. A former director, expired domain, completed contract or revoked licence can mislead an assessment when treated as current.

Sound assessments describe confidence rather than certainty when the evidence remains incomplete. A shared address may indicate a registered agent, a co-working space, a parent company or a shell-company pattern. A shared IP address may indicate common ownership, shared hosting or a content delivery network. A sanctions hit may concern a similarly named entity rather than the target. Analysts reduce these errors by collecting identifiers such as registration numbers, employer identification numbers, tax numbers, LEIs, DUNS or UEI numbers, USCCs, vessel IMO numbers, domain creation dates and unique email domains.

Privacy, safety and proportionality remain important. Public data can include personal details about staff, donors, contractors and vulnerable people. Analysts should collect only what the question requires, protect sensitive selectors and escalate high-risk exposures through responsible channels.
## Corporate structure
Corporate governance separates ownership, supervision and day-to-day management. Shareholders own equity, directors supervise the organisation, and officers manage operations. Large organisations often use complex structures because they operate across countries, markets and regulatory environments. Illicit actors can exploit the same complexity to obscure control, money movement and liability.

Key entity types include:
- Parent company - an entity that controls one or more other entities
- Holding company - an entity that owns controlling interests and may exist mainly to hold assets or shares
- Subsidiary - a separate legal entity controlled by another company
- Wholly owned subsidiary - a subsidiary with all shares controlled by a parent
- Limited liability company - an entity designed to limit owner liability under applicable law
- Beneficial owner - a person who ultimately owns, controls or benefits from an entity or asset
- Intermediary company - an entity positioned between buyers, sellers, owners or assets
- Foreign-registered entity - an entity incorporated in one jurisdiction while operating elsewhere
- Non-profit organisation - an organisation formed for a social, charitable, educational or similar purpose rather than profit distribution

Corporate structures often contain many layers. A global company may have country subsidiaries, regional subsidiaries, branch offices, real estate entities and charitable arms. Analysts map these layers to identify control, exposure, conflicts, sanctions risk and unexplained relationships.
## Official records and company filings
Official registers provide a strong starting point. OpenCorporates aggregates company registry data from many jurisdictions and can reveal incorporation details, alternative names, addresses, officers, branches, subsidiaries, trademarks and recent events. Paid tools such as D&B Hoovers, Dow Jones Risk and Compliance and LexisNexis WorldCompliance may add deeper private-company, sanctions and linkage data, but analysts should still validate important findings against primary sources.

In the United States, the Securities and Exchange Commission was created by the Securities Exchange Act of 1934. EDGAR provides free public access to filings by public companies and other filers. Domestic public companies generally file annual reports on Form 10-K, quarterly reports on Form 10-Q and current reports on Form 8-K for specified material events. Form 10-K is not a simplified annual report. It is a comprehensive annual report filed with audited financial statements and differs from the annual report sent to shareholders.

Annual reports and securities filings can reveal officers, directors, key employees, office locations, financial condition, subsidiaries, partnerships, competitors, legal matters, risk factors and future plans. These filings also support subject intelligence because directors, major shareholders and executives often connect the organisation to other entities, foreign interests, political donors and prior disputes.

Outside the United States, analysts should use the relevant national system. Canada now uses SEDAR+ for issuer filings and securities disclosure. The United Kingdom provides company filings through Companies House and local planning portals for building applications. China publishes business registration data through the National Enterprise Credit Information Publicity System, including the Unified Social Credit Code. Russia's official legal entity register is EGRUL. Iran's Official Gazette and Tehran Chamber of Commerce can support entity research, but transliteration and self-reported data require caution.
## Public-facing disclosures
Organisations publish information through websites, portals, press releases, social media and marketing material. Each disclosure can provide selectors for further research.

Useful website pivots include:
- Organisation history, former names and ownership changes
- Products, customers, manufacturing sites and development processes
- Headquarters, branch offices, laboratories and foreign locations
- Contact pages, staff names, titles and email formats
- News about mergers, acquisitions, funding, contracts and asset transfers
- Partnerships with universities, suppliers, governments or research institutes

University partnerships deserve close attention when an organisation works in defence, dual-use technology or sensitive research. Public announcements, grant material and small business research awards may reveal research themes, funding sources, partner institutions and risk exposure.

Social media adds another layer. Profiles can reveal usernames, verification status, websites, join dates, staff imagery, events, product announcements, conference attendance, partnerships, shareholders and followers. Analysts should treat social media as a lead source rather than proof. Posts often require confirmation through filings, contracts, records or independent reporting.
## Misconduct, lawsuits and regulatory history
Public misconduct records help analysts assess whether an organisation has a pattern of improper conduct. Relevant records include court filings, enforcement actions, agency reports, press releases, settlement documents, Freedom of Information material and regulatory databases.

The Project on Government Oversight's Federal Contractor Misconduct Database is no longer maintained, but its underlying data remains useful for historical contractor research. Good Jobs First's Violation Tracker collates civil and criminal regulatory violations from federal, state and local sources. These resources can reveal discrimination, improper billing, deceptive claims, kickbacks, contamination, theft, wrongful termination, antitrust matters, environmental violations and arms control issues.

Misconduct data supports due diligence because legal actions and penalties can reveal operational risk, weak governance, fraud indicators and reputational exposure. Analysts should verify database entries against the primary record, note the date and distinguish allegations from findings, settlements and convictions.
## Contracts and procurement
Contracts are legally binding agreements that define rights, obligations, services, materials, payment and performance. Government procurement records can reveal spending priorities, suppliers, subcontractors, locations, timelines, technical requirements and attached documents. They can also expose sensitive operational details when agencies publish statements of work, maps, plans, schematics or security requirements.

Common procurement terms include:
- Contractor - a person or organisation that provides goods or services under a contract
- Subcontractor - a party engaged by a contractor to perform part of the work
- Request for proposal - a solicitation that invites proposals and may permit negotiation
- Request for quote - a request for vendors to submit pricing
- Bid - a submission responding to a solicitation
- Scope of work - a description of required services, materials or outcomes
- Statement of work - a detailed description of deliverables, timing and requirements
- Task order - an order for work under an existing contract vehicle
- Teaming - an arrangement where organisations cooperate to pursue or perform work
- NAICS code - an industry classification code
- PSC code - a product or service code used in US federal procurement
- IDIQ - an indefinite-delivery, indefinite-quantity contract vehicle

USAspending.gov is the official source for US federal spending data. SAM.gov publishes US federal contract opportunities, including pre-solicitation notices, solicitations, award notices and sole-source notices. Analysts can use award identifiers, recipients, agencies, addresses, award dates, descriptions, NAICS codes, PSC codes and transaction histories as pivots. Not all contracts, full attachments or classified details appear publicly, so absence of evidence does not prove absence of work.

Contract research also helps security teams see what adversaries might learn. Public records may identify cleaning vendors, construction work, transport providers, security providers or technology purchases. Analysts should report sensitive exposure responsibly and avoid amplifying details that could facilitate physical or cyber intrusion.
## Power mapping
Power mapping identifies people and institutions that influence organisational decisions. Power may come from investors, directors, executives, major customers, regulators, political donors, trade associations, think tanks, councils or community groups.

Analysts use power mapping to understand incentives, pressure points, alliances and conflicts of interest. In political analysis, a map may place actors by their influence and their support or opposition to a policy goal. In organisational intelligence, the same technique can reveal donors, Super PAC links, board interlocks, shared directors, advisory roles and relationships that shape procurement, policy or strategy.

LittleSis provides open data on relationships among people and organisations, including leadership, interlocks and giving. Its data can support leads, but analysts should review the cited sources behind each relationship before making conclusions.
## Organisational crime and shell companies
Organisational crime includes offences committed by people acting for, through or on behalf of an organisation. White-collar schemes can be difficult to detect because actors hide ownership, assets and money flows across entities, banks and jurisdictions. Common crimes include embezzlement, money laundering, securities fraud, sanctions evasion and racketeering.

The Panama Papers and Paradise Papers showed how offshore entities can support secrecy. Offshore ownership and shell companies are not automatically illegal, but they can help conceal beneficial ownership, profits, losses, motives and the movement of funds.

Important risk indicators include:
- Incorporation in a secrecy jurisdiction or tax haven
- A generic or meaningless company name
- No credible website, staff presence or social media footprint
- Generic email addresses or unverifiable contact details
- Partial, false or residential addresses
- A registered agent shared by many unrelated entities
- Owners who do not appear to exist online or appear as straw men
- Activity that does not match assets, staffing, premises or stated purpose

These indicators do not prove illegality. They justify further collection, cross-checking and cautious assessment.
## Sanctions, blacklists and designations
Sanctions are legal measures used to influence behaviour, constrain threats and enforce foreign policy or security objectives. They may target countries, sectors, entities, vessels or individuals. Sanctions can include asset freezes, trade restrictions, travel bans, arms embargoes and commodity restrictions.

The United Nations Security Council has primary responsibility for maintaining international peace and security and maintains sanctions committees and consolidated lists. The Office of Foreign Assets Control, an office of the US Department of the Treasury, administers and enforces US economic and trade sanctions. Analysts working in due diligence, supply chain security, counter-terrorism, counter-proliferation and maritime investigations should monitor relevant lists and understand the reason for each designation.

Designations support organisational intelligence by identifying risky counterparties, front companies, sanctioned vessels, illicit procurement networks and suspicious ownership chains. Maritime research may also use the Paris MoU White, Grey and Black lists, which assess flag performance from inspections and detentions over a rolling three-year period.
## Non-profit organisations
US 501(c)(3) organisations receive tax-exempt status when they operate for recognised charitable, religious, educational, scientific or similar purposes. Non-profit analysis examines governance, revenue, expenses, grants, compensation, related-party transactions, programmes, donors where disclosed and regulatory history.

ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer and the IRS Tax Exempt Organization Search provide access to Form 990-series returns, Form 990-N postcards, determination letters, Publication 78 data and automatic revocation data. The Federal Audit Clearinghouse publishes single audit submissions for non-federal entities that spend enough federal assistance to trigger the audit requirement. Consumer resources such as Charity Navigator can add governance, accountability and advisory context.

Non-profit research should distinguish poor administration from fraud. Analysts should compare filings across years, check whether programmes align with stated purpose, review compensation and contractor relationships, and verify adverse findings through official records.
## Domain registration and IP analysis
Technical infrastructure can reveal how an organisation presents itself online and how its systems connect to other entities. Analysts examine domains, websites, DNS records, registration data, IP addresses, autonomous system numbers and hosting relationships.

A domain name maps human-readable names to technical resources through DNS. Registration data was historically accessed through WHOIS. For generic top-level domains, RDAP is now the definitive protocol for registration data, although many people still use the term WHOIS. Registration records may show registrar, creation dates, expiry dates, nameservers and contact information, but privacy rules, proxy services and redaction often hide personal registrant details.

IP addresses identify network interfaces, not always specific people or devices. Public IP addresses can be reassigned, shared by many users through network address translation, or masked by hosting providers, VPNs, proxies and content delivery networks. Static addresses tend to remain stable. Dynamic addresses can change. Private addresses operate inside local networks.

Analysts can query DNS tools to identify A records, mail records, nameservers and ASNs. Regional Internet registries - AFRINIC, APNIC, ARIN, LACNIC and RIPE NCC - maintain Internet number registrations. RDAP and registry searches can identify an IP range holder, allocation details and contact roles.

Infrastructure analysis requires restraint. Shared hosting, Cloudflare, CloudFront and other content delivery networks can make unrelated websites appear connected. A small number of co-hosted sites, matching registration details, shared nameservers, repeated contact data and corroborating business records strengthen attribution. A single IP overlap rarely proves common ownership.
