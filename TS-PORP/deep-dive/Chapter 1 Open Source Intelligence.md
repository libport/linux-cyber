# Chapter 1: Open Source Intelligence
## Scope and method
Analysts turn publicly or commercially available information into open-source intelligence (OSINT) when they collect, evaluate and enrich that information to answer a defined requirement. Sources include internet data, social media, journalism, public records, subscription databases, audio, video, maps, satellite imagery and other geospatial material.

Lawful OSINT depends on access rights, not on secrecy or intrusion. Analysts may use information available to the public or to ordinary customers, but they do not use exposed credentials, intrude into accounts, run unauthorised scans or trigger account recovery flows without approval. Sound collection minimises operational noise and protects privacy, civil liberties and human rights.

OSINT is not a specialist path reserved for intelligence or military veterans. Journalists, investigators, government staff, educators, business analysts, genealogists and security teams all use OSINT. Strong analysts show curiosity, structure, critical thinking, communication skill, persistence and attention to detail. Technical skills matter, but they do not replace disciplined method. A browser search that finds a subject's published email can be as valuable as a Python workflow that correlates records, provided the result is verified, lawful and fit for purpose. Effective teams value simple, reproducible collection before complex tooling. They also define the objective before collecting, because unfocused searching can create privacy risk, waste time and produce weak assessments.
## Development
US open-source intelligence has deep institutional roots. The Foreign Broadcast Monitoring Service, created in 1941, monitored and translated foreign broadcasts during the Second World War. Its successor, the Foreign Broadcast Information Service, supported Cold War analysis, including reporting during the Cuban Missile Crisis. In 2005, the Director of National Intelligence created the Open Source Center to improve open-source collection, analysis, training and technology across the intelligence community. In 2015, the centre became the Open Source Enterprise.

Social media changed OSINT practice. During the 2009 Iranian Green Movement, user-generated posts helped observers follow events despite restrictions on traditional media. Mobile phones, social platforms, commercial imagery and public databases now give analysts more material than earlier intelligence organisations could access. Encryption and stronger privacy practices reduce some visibility, while open-source software, specialist communities and training expand analyst capability.
## Blending disciplines
Modern OSINT overlaps with other intelligence disciplines. Public satellite imagery supports imagery intelligence, geolocation and geospatial analysis. Social media, public records and data brokers can support work that once relied on human-source collection. Wireless and device data can create signals-like leads when analysts have lawful authority and ethical approval.

This overlap increases both value and risk. Analysts need source evaluation, verification and clear boundaries. They must distinguish publicly available information from stolen, restricted or unlawfully obtained data. They must also record collection steps so another analyst can understand, test and reproduce the work.
## Future tradecraft
The next phase of OSINT will combine linked data, machine-readable web standards, big data, artificial intelligence, machine learning and natural language processing. These technologies can help analysts plan requirements, target collection, process large datasets, detect patterns, assess sentiment and tailor reporting for decision-makers.

Automation will not remove analyst judgement. AI systems can accelerate collection and clustering, but they can also amplify bias, misread context and create false confidence. Deepfakes, synthetic media, propaganda and coordinated disinformation will increase the need for careful verification. Predictive analytics may help identify risks, but law enforcement and intelligence use can also reinforce discrimination when data, models or assumptions are flawed. The most useful future capability will pair machine speed with human scrutiny. Analysts will need to understand how tools select sources, weight signals, handle missing data and explain outputs.
## Critical thinking and bias
Critical thinking gives OSINT its value. Analysts must test facts, evidence, observations and arguments before forming judgements. A structured model helps them define the requirement, identify key questions, state expected evidence, separate inference from fact, expose assumptions, assess source reliability, consider consequences and examine other points of view.

Bias can distort every stage of collection and analysis. Confirmation bias favours information that supports an existing belief. First impression bias gives early evidence too much weight. Outcome bias treats previous results as proof for later decisions. Blind spot bias makes analysts see other people's bias more clearly than their own. Groupthink rewards agreement over accuracy. The law of the hammer pushes analysts towards familiar tools even when a simpler method would work better.

Analysts reduce bias by asking direct questions:
- Why does this source appear credible?
- What evidence would disprove the current assessment?
- Which assumptions shape the collection plan?
- What alternative explanation fits the same facts?
## Reporting and collaboration
Crowdsourcing and community collaboration can scale difficult investigations, especially humanitarian, missing-person and conflict-related work. They require governance. Skilled leads should verify volunteers, define the scope, protect sensitive information and prevent direct contact with subjects. Public enthusiasm is not a substitute for evidence handling, victim care or legal process.

Analysts should keep records of selectors, search paths, collection dates, confidence levels and source limitations. They should separate what a source says from what the analyst infers. This record supports review, reduces duplication and helps decision-makers understand whether an assessment is strong, tentative or contradicted by other evidence.
## Mental health
OSINT work can expose analysts to graphic violence, victim accounts, abuse material, torture, war crimes and sexual exploitation. Repeated exposure can contribute to vicarious trauma, secondary trauma, compassion fatigue, burnout or post-traumatic stress symptoms. Common warning signs include shock, denial, anger, sadness, mood swings, irritability, fatigue, racing heartbeat and withdrawal.

Organisations should treat mental health as an operational risk. They should prepare analysts before difficult work, limit unnecessary exposure, rotate tasks, provide supervision and make professional support normal. Analysts should not treat distress as weakness. Poor mental health can damage judgement, relationships, performance and case outcomes.
## Ethics
OSINT regularly touches privacy, safety and reputation. Doxing, or exposing personal information without consent to cause harm, can lead to harassment, stalking, discrimination, job loss, identity theft, financial harm and physical danger. Crowdsourced investigations can also cause serious harm when untrained participants misidentify people, publish private details or contact suspects.

The 2013 Boston Marathon bombing shows the danger. Online communities wrongly identified Sunil Tripathi as a suspect and exposed his family to harassment before authorities identified Dzhokhar and Tamerlan Tsarnaev. The January 6, 2021 attack on the U.S. Capitol also raised difficult questions about public identification, accountability and privacy.

Ethical OSINT requires lawful authority, proportionate collection and responsible dissemination. Analysts should avoid stolen breach data unless a lawful and approved process permits its use. They should report suspected crimes through appropriate channels rather than publishing accusations or personal details. They should avoid password knocking and similar account interactions unless authorised, because those actions can alert subjects, compromise investigations and cross from passive research into active interference.

OSINT practitioners should seek truth, act with integrity, respect privacy and human rights, prevent unnecessary harm, report wrongdoing through proper channels, share information responsibly and keep improving their tradecraft.

Good reporting separates facts, inferences and confidence levels. It states the requirement, summarises the method, explains source limitations and preserves enough detail for review without exposing unnecessary personal information. Analysts should prefer the least intrusive collection path that can answer the question. When evidence remains uncertain, they should say so clearly rather than overstating a finding.
