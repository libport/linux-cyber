# Chapter 5 Social Media Intelligence
Social media intelligence, or SOCMINT, is the collection, preservation and analysis of information from social platforms. It supports open-source intelligence, criminal investigations, civil litigation, corporate due diligence, insurance inquiries, market entry decisions and reputational risk work. Its value comes from the scale and texture of social data. Profiles, posts, comments, likes, locations, photos, videos and relationships can reveal activities, affiliations, movements, interests and connections that may not appear in conventional records.

Investigators must distinguish public information from restricted information. Public posts, public profile fields, public images, public videos, reviews and open group material may fall within the scope of open-source collection. Private messages, restricted posts, closed groups and non-public account data require lawful authority, consent or another recognised legal basis. Courts, regulators, employers and platform operators may treat access methods differently, so investigators should record the legal basis for collection before gathering, preserving or reporting social data.
## Investigative value
Social platforms can assist an investigation by showing:
- identities, usernames and aliases
- family, friendship, workplace and community links
- education and employment history
- interests, memberships and public affiliations
- images, videos and visual context
- location clues from check-ins, captions, landmarks and metadata
- dates and times of activity
- language, tone and recurring topics
- links to websites, blogs, email addresses and other accounts

The value of these clues depends on verification. Social media content may be inaccurate, staged, outdated, automated, hacked, copied from another source or posted by someone other than the named account holder. Investigators should corroborate social media findings with independent records, archive copies, platform metadata, image analysis, web searches, witness accounts or official documents.
## Content types
Social media evidence usually falls into several practical categories.

- Posts and comments record text, links, images, videos and reactions.
- Replies show direct interaction between users and may reveal relationships, disputes or shared interests.
- Images and videos can show people, places, objects, dates, logos, vehicles, uniforms, documents, events and environmental clues.
- Social connections map relationships between accounts, pages, groups and communities.
- Metadata can show upload times, device context, location data or platform-specific identifiers when available.

Privacy settings limit what an investigator can see, but privacy settings do not guarantee confidentiality. A user may share content with a small audience, then another person may copy, screenshot or repost it publicly. Investigators should treat reposted material carefully and preserve the chain of discovery, because the visible publisher may not be the original source.
## Platform categories
Social media is broader than social networking. The main categories include:
- social networking, such as Facebook and LinkedIn
- photo sharing, such as Instagram and Flickr
- video sharing, such as YouTube and TikTok
- blogs and publishing platforms, such as WordPress and Blogger
- microblogging and short-post platforms, such as X and Tumblr
- forums and discussion boards, such as Reddit and Quora
- social gaming communities
- bookmarking and curation services, such as Pinterest and Pocket
- review platforms, such as Yelp and product review sites
- paste sites and code-sharing sites that may expose credentials, claims, manifestos or leaks
- regional platforms, including Weibo, VK and other country-specific services

Each category produces different evidence. A professional network may show employment history, endorsements and colleagues. A review site may show visits, complaints or consumer behaviour. A forum may reveal specialist knowledge, ideology or aliases. A photo platform may reveal locations and visual timelines. Good SOCMINT work begins with the question under investigation, then selects platforms likely to hold relevant evidence.
## Facebook and Meta platforms
Facebook remains valuable because profiles, pages, groups, events, posts, comments, tags, reactions, Marketplace activity and Messenger-adjacent identity clues can create a detailed social graph. Public profile material may show work, education, relationship information, location history, languages, interests and links to other accounts. Public pages and groups may show memberships, posts, administrators, events, followers and recurring topics.

Investigators should no longer rely on older Facebook Graph Search workflows as if they still operate in their original form. Facebook introduced Graph Search in 2013, but major public-facing Graph Search functions were restricted or removed in 2019. Many older URL patterns and third-party Graph Search generators stopped working or became unreliable. Modern Facebook searching relies more on platform search, search engine indexing, archived pages, page transparency features, lawful requests, screenshots, manual review and specialist tools that comply with current platform and legal limits.

Facebook also restricted search by phone number and email after abuse of those features. A profile may not appear through contact details even when the investigator has the correct number or address. Account search results also vary by privacy settings, location, language, login state, mutual connections and platform ranking.

Images from Facebook may preserve useful identifiers in filenames, but investigators should not rely on a single filename pattern. Facebook processing changes over time, and images often circulate outside their original context. Reverse image search, visual comparison, page archives, embedded metadata, visible landmarks and source account review provide stronger corroboration.

Investigators must avoid false-identity accounts unless a lawful authority, organisational policy and platform terms clearly allow a controlled undercover method. Facebook prohibits impersonation and enforces account integrity rules. Evidence gathered through deceptive access may face admissibility, ethical and disciplinary problems.
## X, formerly Twitter
X is useful for time-sensitive public conversation, breaking news, public statements, hashtags, lists, geolocation clues, repost networks and interaction patterns. Posts can show political views, complaints, threats, claims of responsibility, event attendance, organisational affiliations or contact with other accounts. Public replies and reposts often show stronger evidence of interaction than simple following relationships.

Search should combine names, handles, aliases, hashtags, phrases, links, dates and account operators. Investigators should preserve posts quickly, because users can delete posts, change handles, lock accounts or remove media. Screenshots alone are weak preservation. Better practice captures the URL, handle, user ID where available, timestamp, full thread context, media, surrounding replies and any external links.

The platform has changed substantially since it operated under the Twitter brand. API access and research access have become more restricted and often paid. Older free tools and academic workflows may fail, return partial data or breach current terms. Current investigations should test tools before relying on them and record the tool version, query terms and collection date.
## LinkedIn
LinkedIn is often the best starting point for professional background research. It can help identify employers, roles, dates, education, skills, licences, certifications, endorsements, publications, projects, companies, recruiters and work associates. It can also help map relationships between directors, executives, employees, contractors and former colleagues.

LinkedIn evidence needs caution. Profiles are self-authored and may exaggerate duties, dates, seniority or qualifications. Endorsements and recommendations may reflect friendship or networking rather than verified competence. Investigators should compare LinkedIn claims with corporate registries, professional licensing bodies, archived company pages, court records, regulator databases, recruitment advertisements and other independent material.

Investigators should also account for visibility settings. Viewing a profile may reveal the investigator's account unless privacy settings prevent it. Automated scraping may breach LinkedIn terms and may create legal risk. Manual collection, documented search queries and lawful discovery processes are safer for formal matters.
## Google+ and retired platforms
Some older SOCMINT guides treat Google+ as an active social network. Google shut down the consumer version of Google+ in April 2019 and deleted consumer content from Google+ accounts and pages. Historical references to Google+ now matter mainly for archived material, legacy links, old screenshots, search engine caches, records preserved in litigation or migrated enterprise records.

The same principle applies to any retired, renamed or substantially changed platform. Investigators should not assume that old tools, examples, statistics or interface instructions remain valid. Platform names, privacy controls, public search access, APIs, data retention and account visibility change regularly.
## Search and collection methods
Effective SOCMINT uses layered searching rather than a single platform search box. Investigators should combine:
- platform search for names, usernames, hashtags, phrases and places
- search engine operators such as site-specific searches and exact phrases
- reverse image search for profile photos and reused images
- username enumeration across multiple platforms
- archived web pages and cached search results
- page, group and account transparency features
- timeline reconstruction from posts, comments, images and event pages
- network mapping of visible relationships
- language, time zone and location analysis
- metadata review where lawful and available

Search terms should include spelling variants, nicknames, former names, transliterations, email prefixes, phone fragments, company names, domain names, school names, club names and known associates. Investigators should keep a search log showing dates, platforms, queries, accounts used, results found and pages preserved.
## Preservation and evidence handling
Social media evidence is volatile. Users can delete posts, alter privacy settings, change usernames, edit captions, remove comments or close accounts. Platforms can also remove material for policy reasons. Investigators should preserve relevant material as soon as it is found.

A sound preservation record includes:
- the URL and platform
- the account name, username and visible user identifier
- the date and time of access with time zone
- screenshots of the page and surrounding context
- saved copies of images, videos and linked pages where lawful
- visible timestamps and engagement metrics
- the collection method and tool used
- hash values for exported files when evidence handling requires them
- notes explaining how the material was found

For litigation or law enforcement work, investigators should follow the relevant rules of evidence, platform law enforcement guidelines, privacy law and organisational policy. A court order, subpoena, warrant, consent process or formal preservation request may be required for non-public account records.
## Analysis and interpretation
SOCMINT analysis should separate observation from inference. A public post may prove that an account published a statement at a time, but it may not prove who typed it, whether the person was at the tagged location or whether the statement was true. A check-in may show a claimed location, while an image may provide stronger location evidence if landmarks, weather, shadows, signage and metadata align.

Social graphs also need careful interpretation. A friend, follower, contact or endorser is not necessarily a close associate. A like may indicate approval, bookmarking, irony, monitoring or accidental interaction. A repost may support, criticise or simply draw attention to a claim. Strong conclusions require repeated interactions, mutual context and corroboration.

Language analysis can identify mood, topic, risk signals or behavioural change, but automated tone and personality tools are not proof. They reflect model assumptions, training data and platform bias. Investigators should use them as leads, not conclusions.
## Ethical and legal limits
SOCMINT can expose sensitive details about private lives, beliefs, health, children, relationships and locations. Investigators should collect only what is necessary and proportionate to the matter. They should avoid unnecessary intrusion into family members, friends and unrelated third parties. Reports should include relevant evidence, explain limitations and omit prejudicial material that does not answer the investigative question.

Employers, insurers, lenders and corporate investigators should take particular care when using social media for screening or decision-making. Public social content can reveal protected attributes such as religion, disability, ethnicity, age, pregnancy, political views or union activity. Using those details improperly may create discrimination, privacy or procedural fairness risks.
## Conclusion
Social media intelligence helps investigators locate people, reconstruct events, test claims, identify networks and discover leads. Its strength lies in combining public records, platform activity, visual evidence, metadata and careful chronology. Its weakness lies in volatility, platform change, privacy limits, false accounts, altered content and overconfident inference. Reliable SOCMINT therefore depends on lawful access, prompt preservation, careful verification, transparent methods and conservative conclusions.
