# Chapter 6 People Search Engines and Public Records
## Purpose and scope
Most people now leave a digital footprint through social platforms, business directories, property records, court systems, job sites, data brokers and breached-data indexes. People search engines exploit that footprint by aggregating records that would otherwise sit across many separate systems. They can help an investigator identify likely addresses, aliases, relatives, phone numbers, email addresses, usernames, employment history and public affiliations. They can also mislead. Aggregators often merge records for people with similar names, keep stale addresses, exaggerate criminal or financial links and present scraped data without context.

Responsible OSINT treats these services as leads, not proof. A result gains value only when independent sources confirm it. Official registers, court portals, direct documents and timestamped public records carry more weight than a commercial report. Sensitive findings need stricter handling because a mistaken match can damage a person unfairly.
## Search preparation
An effective people search starts with known identifiers: full name, aliases, age range, current or former locations, email address, phone number, username, occupation, education history, family links and business relationships. The investigator records source, date accessed, search term and result uncertainty for each lead. This audit trail supports later verification and helps separate a confirmed identity from a similar-name false positive. Searches should move from public, low-intrusion sources to more sensitive records only when the purpose justifies the step.
## Public records and legal limits
Public records are government-held records that law allows the public to inspect, request or buy in limited ways. Access differs sharply by jurisdiction. The United States has broad federal freedom of information rights, but many personal records remain restricted, redacted or held at state, county or local level. Australia, the United Kingdom and many other jurisdictions also provide freedom of information rights, usually balanced against privacy, security and confidentiality exemptions. The United States does not stand alone in allowing public-record access.

Public records can include births, deaths, marriages, divorces, property ownership, business registrations, professional licences, court filings, bankruptcy records, electoral finance records, patents, tax-related property information and some criminal justice records. Birth, death and marriage records are not always online, not always public and not always centralised. Older records may exist only in local archives, religious registers or paper files. Some records may require proof of relationship, consent or a formal application.
## Main search categories
People search engines combine public records, commercial data, social profiles and address histories. Examples include services focused on names, reverse phone lookup, reverse address lookup and email lookup. Many are paid, US-centric and unsuitable for employment, tenancy, credit, insurance or other eligibility decisions unless they comply with consumer-reporting law. Their value lies in triage, not final judgment.

Vital-record and genealogy sources help establish identity, family relationships and life events. They can confirm dates and places, but indexes often omit full certificates and can confuse people with similar names.

Court and criminal-record searches require extra caution. Official court, prison and sex-offender registries are more reliable than aggregator summaries. Records may show allegations, charges, dismissed matters or historic offences that need legal context. The investigator should verify jurisdiction, record type, date, disposition and subject identity before drawing any conclusion.

Property, tax and corporate records can reveal addresses, ownership interests, liens, director roles and business associations. These sources can be strong when official, but they may not identify residents, beneficial owners or informal control without further corroboration.

Usernames, email addresses and phone numbers link online activity across platforms. Reverse lookup tools, social-network search, domain records, breach-notification services and email-header analysis can connect accounts to a person or organisation. Technical artefacts should be handled carefully because shared devices, recycled numbers and forwarded emails can create false links.

Online registries and wish lists can reveal life events such as weddings, births, graduations and gift preferences. They may also expose relatives and friends who interact with the registry. These sources need restraint because people often leave them public by habit rather than informed consent. A relevant registry can corroborate a relationship or address, but unrelated preferences should normally be ignored.
## Breached data and security checks
Breach repositories can show whether an email address, phone number or password hash appears in known breaches. They should support defensive security and exposure assessment, not account access. Using leaked credentials to enter another person's account is illegal and unethical. Modern checking services can reduce risk by using hash-based or verified workflows that avoid sending complete passwords in plain text.
## Work, social and relationship sources
Job sites, professional networks, corporate pages and resumes can reveal employment history, skills, technologies in use and organisational structure. Dating sites, registries, hobby groups and event platforms may reveal relationships and interests, but they also create high privacy risk. The investigator should collect only information relevant to a legitimate purpose and avoid unnecessary intrusion into intimate or unrelated details.
## Verification, ethics and privacy
Good OSINT weighs reliability, legality, necessity and proportionality. The investigator verifies names against multiple identifiers, distinguishes current from historic data, checks whether a service is authorised for the intended use and records uncertainty. Commercial people-search results, scraped profiles and leaked data should never replace official records, consent-based checks or lawful background-screening processes.

Individuals can reduce exposure by reviewing major people-search sites, using opt-out tools, tightening social media privacy settings, deleting abandoned accounts, using unique passwords, enabling multi-factor authentication and monitoring breach alerts. Removal is rarely permanent because data brokers refresh records from public and commercial sources. Privacy protection therefore works best as periodic maintenance.
