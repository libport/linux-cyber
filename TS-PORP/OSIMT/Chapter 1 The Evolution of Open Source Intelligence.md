# Chapter 1: The Evolution of Open Source Intelligence
## Definition and scope
Open source intelligence (OSINT) is intelligence derived from publicly or commercially available information to answer a defined intelligence requirement. Public availability does not make raw material intelligence. Data becomes OSINT only after collection, evaluation, filtering, analysis and dissemination to an appropriate audience.

OSINT can draw from online and offline sources. Common sources include websites, social media, forums, news media, public records, company reports, academic work, conference material, commercial imagery, maps, photographs, videos, metadata and specialist databases. Dark web material can form part of OSINT only when it is collected lawfully and without unauthorised access. Copyright, privacy law, contractual terms and ethical duties still apply, even when material is visible to the public.
## Evolution
Modern OSINT grew from wartime broadcast monitoring. The United States began foreign broadcast monitoring in 1941, and this work became the Foreign Broadcast Information Service in 1942. After the 11 September 2001 attacks, reviews of intelligence capability gave OSINT greater institutional priority. The CIA Open Source Center replaced FBIS in 2005, and CIA open source work is now associated with the Open Source Enterprise. The field has since expanded beyond government into law enforcement, business, journalism, security research and civil society.

Digital communication has made OSINT more valuable and more difficult. The internet, social platforms, commercial data services, sensors and connected devices produce large volumes of material. Analysts gain reach and speed, but they must manage duplication, stale content, misinformation, source bias and poor provenance.
## Categories of open source material
- Open source data is raw material from a primary source, such as an image, recording, dataset, satellite image or event log.
- Open source information is data that has been organised, edited or filtered, such as a book, article, broadcast report, market report or academic paper.
- Open source intelligence is assessed information that has been selected, processed and communicated for a specific intelligence purpose.
- Validated OSINT is OSINT with a high degree of confidence, either because an authoritative open source supports it or because it has been checked against other trusted sources.

Data, information and knowledge should not be treated as identical. Data records facts. Information gives those facts context. Knowledge combines information with experience, judgement and tested assumptions.

Grey literature includes reports, dissertations, standards, technical papers, newsletters, market surveys, conference papers and other material outside ordinary bookstore or news channels. It can have high intelligence value because it often contains detail that is public in a legal sense but difficult to discover. Paid databases and specialist vendors help locate this material, but licence conditions still control access and reuse.
## Main users
Governments use OSINT to support national security, diplomacy, counterterrorism, crisis awareness, policy development and public sentiment analysis. International organisations use it for peacekeeping, humanitarian response, supply chain protection and risk monitoring. NATO has treated OSINT as a useful foundation for shared understanding because it can often be distributed more easily than classified intelligence.

Law enforcement agencies use OSINT to support investigations into fraud, identity theft, online abuse, counterfeiting, copyright infringement and organised crime. Businesses use it for market intelligence, competitor monitoring, due diligence, threat intelligence and brand protection. Defence firms, financial investigators, journalists, researchers and privacy-conscious individuals also rely on OSINT.

Penetration testers and security teams use OSINT during reconnaissance to understand exposed assets, leaked credentials, staff details and attack surfaces before testing security controls. Criminals and hostile groups can use similar techniques for social engineering, targeting, propaganda and operational planning. This dual use makes lawful authority, purpose limitation and professional ethics essential.
## Collection approaches
Passive collection gathers information from public sources without interacting directly with the target system. It is the preferred approach for most OSINT because it reduces exposure and keeps collection closer to the legal and ethical core of the discipline.

Semi-passive collection sends limited, ordinary-looking traffic to a target to obtain general information. Active collection directly interacts with target systems through scanning, probing or social engineering. These methods belong more clearly to technical reconnaissance and security testing. They can trigger logs, intrusion detection systems and legal risk if performed without authority.
## Benefits
OSINT is often cheaper, faster and less operationally risky than covert human or technical collection. It can answer questions that do not justify classified collection. It can be shared across agencies, allies, companies and public interest groups more easily than secret intelligence. It also supports all-source analysis by adding context, early warning, leads and validation.

OSINT is especially useful when rapid visibility matters. It can help identify emerging crises, public narratives, supply chain exposure, fraud patterns, cyber risk, counterfeit products and reputational threats. Small organisations can also use OSINT effectively because many sources and tools are inexpensive.
## Limits, law and ethics
OSINT has serious limits. Public material may be false, manipulated, outdated, incomplete or deliberately seeded to mislead analysts. Large volumes of data can overwhelm collection teams and automated tools can amplify errors. Human review remains necessary for source assessment, relevance, bias detection and proportionality.

Public accessibility does not remove legal or ethical constraints. Analysts should avoid unauthorised access, unnecessary personal data collection, deceptive engagement and careless republication of harmful material. Leaked classified documents and exposed private records may be reachable online, but that does not automatically make them lawful or ethical OSINT sources.

Privacy is a central risk. Technical identifiers such as IP addresses, device details, browser information and location data may become personal information when combined with other datasets. Analysts should minimise collection, protect stored material, document provenance and explain uncertainty.
## Operational security
OSINT collection leaves traces. Websites, platforms and service providers may log search terms, account identifiers, IP addresses, device fingerprints and browsing behaviour. Criminal organisations, hostile states and capable private actors may detect interest in their activities. OSINT practitioners therefore need operational security, controlled identities, safe browsing environments, secure communications and regular checks of their own digital footprint.
