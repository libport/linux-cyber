# Chapter 9: Investigative Method and Technique: Theft/Property Offenses
## Core impact
Property offences damage more than stock levels. Theft, fraud, burglary and related offending reduce profit, increase insurance and security costs, consume management time, weaken staff morale, affect public safety and may force small businesses to close. Retailers pass some of these costs to consumers through higher prices, while governments lose revenue and fund policing, prosecution, courts and correctional responses.

Retail shrinkage covers preventable loss from customer theft, employee dishonesty, supplier fraud, administrative error, damage and waste. Reported values differ because organisations use different measures, such as replacement cost, inventory cost, book value or retail value. Loss figures should therefore guide risk assessment rather than stand as exact comparisons.

Australian practice should treat retail theft as both an economic problem and a safety problem. The Australian Bureau of Statistics reported that other theft reached 595,660 victims in 2024, with 45 per cent occurring at retail locations. Retail crime also creates confrontation risks for frontline workers, customers and security staff.

A sound program also distinguishes loss categories. Customer theft, employee theft, vendor fraud and administrative error require different remedies. Treating all shrinkage as shoplifting produces poor controls because it ignores receiving errors, refund manipulation, stock count failures, collusion and weak access management. Investigators should compare reported incidents with stock variances, refund patterns, cash discrepancies and staff rosters before drawing conclusions.
## Retail theft methods
Retail theft ranges from low-value concealment to organised offending. Common methods include:
- Palming small items and hiding them in the hand, clothing, bags or prams.
- Price switching, barcode switching, cap switching and false markdowns.
- Concealing items inside boxed goods, shopping bags, umbrellas, cases or loose clothing.
- Removing tags, hiding goods for later recovery or moving stock to blind spots.
- Refund fraud using stolen goods, discarded receipts or false claims of mistaken purchase.
- Steaming, where a group enters a store to intimidate, distract or overwhelm staff.
- Staff collusion, where an employee ignores theft, under-rings goods or assists an offender.
- Organised retail crime, where offenders target goods that resell quickly online or through informal markets.

Security teams should avoid treating every unusual action as theft. Indicators gain value when several observations form a coherent pattern. A person wearing a heavy coat in warm weather, carrying empty bags, repeatedly scanning for staff, moving between blind spots, loitering near exits, handling many goods without interest or creating a distraction may warrant closer observation. The investigator still needs evidence of conduct, intent and loss before any intervention.

Observation should focus on conduct rather than appearance, age, gender, ethnicity or social status. A fair investigation records what a person did, where the person moved, what item was handled, how the item was treated and what opportunity existed to pay. This protects innocent customers, strengthens admissible evidence and reduces reputational risk for the retailer.
## Motives and rationalisations
Shoplifters and dishonest employees often minimise their conduct. Common rationalisations include claiming the act is borrowing, compensation for poor treatment, revenge, a response to hardship, a political act, a workplace norm or a victimless offence. These explanations do not remove criminality, but they help investigators understand risk, design prevention measures and conduct interviews.

Motives usually fall into four broad groups:
- Economic pressure or perceived need.
- Opportunity, temptation or weak controls.
- Revenge, resentment or workplace grievance.
- Ego, thrill seeking, peer influence or disordered thinking.

The strongest prevention programs reduce opportunity and challenge rationalisation. Clear policies, visible staff, fair employment practices, reliable stock control and consistent consequences all help to lower risk.
## Prevention through store design and staff practice
Retail theft prevention starts with the environment. Good design improves natural surveillance and removes easy concealment. A store should keep displays tidy, keep exits visible, place high-value goods away from doors, use locked cabinets or dummy display units for attractive small goods and avoid shelving that blocks staff sightlines.

Practical controls include:
- Greet customers promptly to show staff awareness.
- Train employees to recognise common theft methods without profiling customers unfairly.
- Use electronic article surveillance, ink tags and secure tag removal devices.
- Monitor blind spots through mirrors, CCTV, floor walking and better lighting.
- Keep registers, returns counters, stockrooms and loading areas supervised.
- Check high-risk items that could conceal smaller goods.
- Display bag-check policies, while recognising that bag checks in Australia must be voluntary and staff should not touch a customer's body or possessions.
- Record incidents consistently so management can identify products, times, locations and methods at higher risk.
Investigative method and technique in insurance cases
Technology assists prevention, but it does not replace staff judgement. CCTV, electronic tags, alarms, point-of-sale analytics and exception reporting work best when staff understand how offenders defeat controls.

A prevention program should also reduce the value of stolen goods after they leave the premises. Serial-number recording, asset marking, controlled warranty records, secure online marketplace monitoring and cooperation with police make resale harder. This market reduction approach matters because organised theft depends on a buyer, handler or resale channel.
## Shoplifting investigation
A shoplifting investigation should proceed from observation to evidence, then to intervention. Security staff should not detain a person on suspicion alone. They should establish what the person selected, how it was concealed or altered, whether the person kept control of the item, whether payment was avoided and whether the person passed a payment point or otherwise showed intent to leave without paying.

Investigators should:
- Observe continuously where possible.
- Record time, location, item description, price and staff involved.
- Preserve CCTV and point-of-sale records quickly.
- Keep physical evidence in its original condition.
- Mark, photograph and log recovered items.
- Document the chain of custody from recovery to police, insurer, employer or court.
- Distinguish facts from assumptions in every report.

Evidence has two main forms. Eyewitness evidence shows what the investigator, staff member or camera observed. Physical evidence includes the goods, packaging, tags, receipts, bags, tools, footage and transaction records. A suspect's explanation may also matter because it can support or undermine intent, but interviews must stay fair, calm and lawful.

Detention, recovery and police referral should follow written policy. Staff should consider safety first, especially where a person appears aggressive, intoxicated, armed or supported by others. Civil recovery or disciplinary action should never take priority over preventing injury. When evidence is incomplete, the safer and more defensible decision may be to document the event, preserve footage and continue monitoring for a pattern.
## Internal theft
Employee theft often causes major loss because staff understand systems, blind spots and routines. Internal theft may involve cash, stock, information, refunds, gift cards, discounts, expenses, supplier payments, payroll or records. Methods include under-ringing sales, voiding legitimate transactions, issuing false refunds, giving unauthorised discounts, moving goods through rubbish or loading docks, falsifying records, colluding with vendors, stealing returns and manipulating online or mobile checkout processes.

Warning signs require careful assessment. A drawer that is regularly short, regularly over or always exact may justify review. Repeated no-sale transactions, frequent voids, unusual refunds, changing work patterns, insistence on working alone, friends using one employee's checkout, unexplained lifestyle changes, missing stock near a workstation and unusual vehicles near back doors may all support further inquiry. None proves theft on its own.

Strong internal controls reduce opportunity. Effective controls include separation of duties, rotation of duties, restricted access, surprise audits, password controls, authorisation limits, transaction monitoring, key registers, stock counts, secure returns areas and monitored employee entrances. Management culture also matters. Poor supervision, unclear expectations, unfair treatment and inconsistent discipline can increase rationalisation and opportunity.
## Employee interviews and legal care
Interviewing a suspected employee requires preparation and restraint. The investigator should assemble records, CCTV, stock data, witness accounts and relevant policies before the meeting. The interview should occur in private, with an appropriate management or human resources witness. The investigator should ask clear, non-leading questions and give the employee an opportunity to respond.

Australian investigators should not rely on United States concepts such as Miranda warnings. Local law, evidence rules, employment obligations and workplace surveillance rules govern interviews and detention. Private security staff should avoid threats, promises, coercion, locked rooms, guards at the door, excessive force and statements that suggest the employee cannot leave. Police should handle matters involving violence, serious indictable offending, weapons, unsafe confrontation or any situation beyond the organisation's lawful authority.

Reports should record the allegation, evidence reviewed, questions asked, answers given, documents obtained, items recovered, decisions made and any action taken. The report should avoid legal conclusions unless qualified legal advisers provide them.

Management should separate investigation from punishment. Investigators find and preserve facts. Managers decide employment action under policy, contract and workplace law. This separation helps avoid confirmation bias, protects procedural fairness and improves the quality of decisions.
## Burglary and break-and-enter
Modern Australian burglary and break-and-enter offences are statutory, not fixed by old common law requirements such as night-time entry. In Victoria, burglary involves entering a building, or part of a building, as a trespasser with intent to steal, commit an assault-related offence or commit a relevant property damage offence. Other states and territories use different wording, so investigators should apply the local offence elements.

Burglary investigation focuses on entry, intent, damage, tools, movement through the premises and loss. The investigator should secure the scene, limit contamination, photograph points of entry, identify tool marks, locate footprints or tyre marks, preserve CCTV and alarm data, obtain a detailed list of missing property and interview people with access. If a safe was attacked, the investigator should photograph it to scale and note make, model, size, weight and serial number where available. Explosives, weapons and hazardous materials should be left for police or specialist units.

Common vulnerabilities include poor lighting, unsecured doors, weak locks, exposed hinges, unprotected windows, overgrown shrubs, accessible roofs, unmonitored loading docks, poor key control and outdated alarms. A prevention program should address these vulnerabilities before an incident occurs.

Business burglary prevention should combine physical hardening with routine discipline. Strong locks fail when staff prop open rear doors, share keys, ignore alarm faults or leave high-value goods near exits. The best controls are simple, visible and audited. Lighting should support CCTV and patrols. Doors, safes and shutters should be checked at closing. Alarm activations should be reviewed rather than dismissed as nuisance events.
## Security surveys and loss testing
A security survey identifies vulnerabilities across the exterior grounds, lighting, doors, windows, parking areas, roof access, mechanical rooms, safes, alarms, stockrooms, key control and staff procedures. The survey should produce a permanent record of risks, corrective actions, responsible persons and review dates. It should also assess how people actually use the site, not only how policies say they should use it.

Retail investigators can use controlled testing when policy and law allow it. Honesty testing examines whether employees correctly record purchases, issue receipts, handle change and follow register procedures. Service testing measures approach, courtesy, product knowledge, sales process and compliance with customer service expectations. These tests should be planned, documented and proportionate. They should never encourage an employee to offend or create an unfair trap.

Useful test types include:
- Combination buys, where the investigator buys two or more different items and checks whether all items are recorded.
- Double buys, where a first purchase identifies the register or employee and a second immediate purchase tests whether the next sale is recorded.
- Exchange buys, where the investigator changes from a lower-priced to a higher-priced item and checks whether the price difference is recorded.
- Refund buys, where the investigator tests compliance with refund procedures, especially receipt requirements.
## Missing property and chain of custody
Accurate records turn loss events into usable intelligence. A missing property report should capture the owner, item description, brand, model, serial number, value, location, date discovered, last known location, reporting person and recovery status. A theft report should add suspected method, witnesses, CCTV references, police report details, recommendations and corrective action. A property control receipt should track every transfer of recovered property.

Loss reporting helps organisations identify trends across departments, shifts, products and sites. It also supports insurance, police referral, disciplinary action and civil recovery. Without consistent records, an organisation may recognise loss only after it has become systemic.
## Operational principles
Effective theft and property investigations rely on disciplined routines:
- Know the site, staff, stock and normal movement patterns.
- Analyse loss data by time, place, product, method and person with access.
- Treat businesses, staff and customers as legitimate victims of property crime and violence.
- Prevent through design, supervision, training and technology.
- Investigate through evidence, not hunches.
- Preserve chain of custody for all property, footage and records.
- Escalate to police when safety, seriousness or legal authority requires it.
- Review every incident to improve controls and reduce repeat offending.

Private security contributes most when it prevents loss before it occurs, records evidence when it does occur and supports lawful action without escalating risk.

Post-incident review closes the loop. The review should ask how the offender entered, what drew the offender to the target, which controls failed, which controls worked, how staff responded and what change will prevent repetition. A review that produces no corrective action leaves the same vulnerability available to the next offender.
