# Chapter 4: Witness Testimony and Evidence in the Private Security Industry
## Purpose of witnesses
Witness evidence gives private security investigations practical force. Witnesses identify suspects, describe events, explain surrounding conditions, reveal motive, test early theories and help investigators exclude remote or unlikely suspects. A witness may provide a formal statement, an informal account, a deposition, an admission, an expert report or documentary material that records what a person has seen, heard, done or analysed. Testimony also shapes the direction of an inquiry. It can reveal where to search, which documents matter, which physical items require preservation and which explanations no longer fit the facts.

Investigators should treat witnesses as evidence sources from the start of an assignment. At a scene, they must first address safety and first aid, then identify people who may know what happened. They should ask witnesses to remain available, record names and contact details, separate direct observations from assumptions and preserve notes made as soon as practicable. Witness testimony can be as important as physical or forensic evidence, but it still needs testing.
## Main witness categories
Witnesses commonly fall into lay, expert, missing or unknown and character categories. A single person may fit more than one practical category, but the legal use of their evidence depends on the jurisdiction and the purpose for which the evidence is offered. The practical label should not obscure the evidentiary task. Investigators need to know what the witness can prove, how the witness knows it and what weaknesses may affect the account.

Lay witnesses give evidence based on what they perceived. They may describe a collision, a conversation, a person's apparent intoxication, an estimate of speed or the sequence of events. A lay witness is not an expert just because their evidence seems useful. Lay opinion remains limited to impressions rationally based on perception and helpful to understanding the facts.

Expert witnesses use specialised knowledge gained through training, study, experience, skill or education. They may explain technical evidence, evaluate causation, test a hypothesis, interpret specialist records or help decide whether a claim has scientific or practical merit. They may come from practice, academia or expert consulting work. Experts may assist before litigation by screening cases and identifying weak assumptions, even if their evidence is never used in court.

Character witnesses give evidence about reputation or character. Their evidence can support or undermine credibility, but investigators should treat it cautiously because closeness, loyalty, financial interest and community gossip can distort it. Character evidence does not replace factual proof.
## Locating missing or unknown witnesses
Investigators should not assume that a witness will be easy to find. Some witnesses move without leaving clear contact details. Others avoid contact because of family, debt, criminal or personal pressures. Lawful witness location work can include:
- Interviewing the client for addresses, routines, associates and habits
- Reviewing police reports, incident reports, pleadings and claim documents
- Canvassing the area with a photograph or accurate description
- Checking lawful public records, directories, media reports and open-source material
- Contacting transport providers, businesses or community organisations when relevant
- Using certified mail or other lawful address verification methods
- Engaging specialist tracing services where the work is licensed, lawful and proportionate

Investigators must comply with privacy, surveillance, trespass, impersonation and database access laws. They should not use unlawful pretexts, restricted law enforcement systems or confidential government records. For example, the National Crime Information Center is a US law enforcement resource, not a general private-investigator search tool. Australian investigators also need to work within state and territory licensing, privacy and surveillance-device limits. When a lawyer instructs the investigator, formal processes such as subpoenas, witness summonses and discovery may provide lawful access to material that the investigator could not obtain independently.
## Testing lay evidence
Lay evidence can advance an investigation by confirming facts, producing leads and eliminating suspects. It can also mislead. Memory changes over time, perception varies with stress, lighting, distance, intoxication, age, attention and expectation, and eyewitness identification can be unreliable.

Investigators should corroborate lay accounts wherever possible. They should compare statements with physical evidence, timing records, photographs, video, digital metadata, documents and other witnesses. They should record exact words where precision matters, ask open questions before focused questions and distinguish direct observation from inference. A witness who says a car was moving "fast" gives an impression. An expert or instrument may be needed to estimate speed with technical precision.
## Using expert evidence
Expert evidence helps fact finders understand evidence that lies beyond ordinary knowledge. In US federal courts, Rule 702 now requires the proponent of expert testimony to show that admissibility is more likely than not. The evidence must help the fact finder, rest on sufficient facts or data, use reliable principles and methods and reflect reliable application to the case. Rule 701 keeps lay opinion separate from scientific, technical or other specialised knowledge.

Daubert replaced the old general-acceptance test in US federal courts with a flexible reliability inquiry for scientific evidence. Kumho Tire extended that gatekeeping role to technical and other specialised expert evidence. The result is not automatic acceptance of novel science. Courts still scrutinise relevance, reliability, methodology, testing, peer review, known or potential error rates, standards controlling the method and fit with the facts. The court's task concerns admissibility. The investigator's task begins earlier, with practical reliability and case value.

Australian uniform evidence law uses a different structure. Opinion evidence is generally excluded unless an exception applies. Expert opinion may be admissible where the witness has specialised knowledge based on training, study or experience and the opinion is wholly or substantially based on that knowledge. Investigators should therefore treat admissibility as jurisdiction-specific and seek legal direction before assuming that a method, device or expert opinion will be usable in court.

Polygraph evidence illustrates the need for caution. Polygraphs may have investigative uses in some settings, but courts often treat them sceptically and employment use is restricted in the United States. Investigators should not present polygraph results as reliable proof of truthfulness without legal and scientific support.
## Screening and selecting experts
Credentials matter, but they do not prove reliability by themselves. Investigators should examine whether an expert's qualifications match the precise issue. A degree in one field does not qualify a witness to opine in another. The spelling and context of technical matters also matter. Bendectin, for example, is a drug name. A proposed expert on its effects would need relevant pharmacological, toxicological, teratological or medical expertise, not only general medical training.

Useful screening questions include whether the expert can identify the facts relied on, state the method used, explain why that method is reliable, describe testing or peer review, disclose error rates where known, address alternative explanations and show that the opinion follows from the method rather than from advocacy. Useful screening criteria include:
- Relevant practical experience in the specific field
- Appropriate education, training, licensing and certification
- Current professional memberships and continuing education
- Peer-reviewed publications, textbooks, presentations or recognised technical work
- Previous expert testimony and performance under cross-examination
- Prior work for each side of litigation and possible patterns of bias
- Clear methods for conflict checking and record keeping
- Ability to explain limits, assumptions and error risks in plain language

Experts can be found through universities, professional associations, scholarly literature, specialist directories, consulting firms, trade schools and experienced local practitioners. Private expert agencies can help locate and screen specialists, but investigators should not outsource judgement. They should verify the expert's current credentials, field fit, independence and method.
## Expert retainers and bias control
Expert services require clear written terms. A retainer should specify the work, hourly or fixed fees, minimum charges, travel costs, report timing, testimony charges, document handling, cancellation terms, confidentiality, conflicts and payment conditions. Contingency fees for expert testimony can damage independence and may be unlawful or unethical in some contexts.

Payment creates at least a perceived risk of bias. Investigators should not assume that an expert favours the paying party, but they should disclose and manage financial relationships. The best expert helps the investigator understand the evidence, including weaknesses.
## Processing witnesses
Witness handling depends on respectful communication. Friendly witnesses may cooperate quickly. Reluctant or adverse witnesses may need a clear explanation of the investigator's role, authority, purpose and requested information. Letters and forms should be plain, courteous and non-threatening. They should ask for what the witness saw, heard and knows about other witnesses, and they should avoid overstating the client's case.

Investigators should preserve the integrity of every witness contact. They should keep accurate notes, record dates and times, identify who was present, avoid leading questions, avoid promises they cannot keep and protect confidential information. Investigators should also evaluate demeanour carefully. Nervousness, confidence, fluency and emotion can affect how a witness presents, but they do not prove truth or falsity. Strong witness work turns scattered accounts into evidence that can be checked, weighed and used responsibly.
