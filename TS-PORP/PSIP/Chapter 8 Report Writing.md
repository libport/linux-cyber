# Chapter 8: Report Writing
Private investigators rely on reports to record what they did, what they observed, what they found and what remains unresolved. Report writing supports the investigation from the first instruction to the final advice to the client. Clear records protect historical accuracy, help the investigator test the available facts and give supervisors, colleagues, lawyers and clients a common account of the case.

Good reports use plain language. They do not dress simple facts in complex wording. They state what happened, when it happened, who was involved, where it happened, how it was discovered and what action followed. They separate observation from inference. They also avoid unsupported opinions, emotional language and vague descriptions.
## Why reports matter
Reports improve efficiency because they stop investigators repeating completed work. A current activity note, incident report or progress report shows the next investigator what has already occurred, what evidence exists and what enquiries remain open. Shared documentation also helps a team work from one version of the facts rather than competing memories or informal assumptions.

Reports strengthen memory. Human recall fades and changes under pressure, delay and discussion. Notes taken at the scene and reports prepared soon after the event preserve detail while it remains fresh. Later readers can then answer questions from documented facts rather than relying on recollection.

Reports organise the investigation. Written records force investigators to arrange events in time order, catalogue people and property and explain the links between evidence, enquiries and findings. A firm also uses records to manage assignments, expenses, personnel, equipment, intelligence, photographs, evidence, statistics and action taken.

Reports prove services rendered. Private security and investigation firms must show what work they performed and why they charged for it. A report gives the client evidence of progress and explains the value of the service. It may also defend the firm if a client later alleges delay, poor performance or non-performance.
## Standards for authorship
An investigative report should be accurate, brief and complete. Accuracy requires factual, legal and technical correctness. Brevity requires concise wording without sacrificing material detail. Completeness requires enough information to support later review, litigation, management action or further investigation.

A reliable report should:
- Record events in chronological order
- Identify known people by name
- Use neutral labels when names are unknown
- List property, vehicles, witnesses and subjects consistently across all relevant sections
- Record the value of missing or damaged property where relevant
- Summarise each statement and identify who made it
- Use detailed descriptions for suspects, missing persons and key evidence
- Use general descriptions in the narrative where detailed fields already capture the specifics
- Record future contact details for witnesses and complainants where lawful and necessary
- Explain gaps, uncertainty and unresolved issues

Investigators should describe behaviour rather than state unsupported conclusions. A report should not say that a child seemed afraid when the observed facts can be recorded instead, such as silence, crying, withdrawal or clinging to another person. Concrete observations are more useful and more defensible than impressions.

Investigators should review every report before submission. The final version should be factual, clear, accurate, objective, grammatically correct, legible, concise and complete. Grammar and spelling matter because poor expression can create ambiguity, weaken credibility and expose the report writer to cross-examination.
## Field notes
Field notes provide the foundation for the formal report. They capture the investigator's first observations, questions, times, locations and actions. They are less polished than the final report, but they still require care because later reports often depend on them.

Effective field notes usually record:
- Date and time of notification
- Time of arrival and departure
- Exact location
- Weather, lighting and scene conditions
- Names and contact details of relevant people
- Statements made at or near the scene
- People who entered, moved, touched or altered anything
- Photographs, sketches and digital records made
- Physical evidence found, by whom it was found and who took custody
- Actions by police, emergency services, forensic staff and other investigators

Field notes should follow the sequence of the investigation. Each page should identify the investigator, date and case. Notes should preserve facts, not polish them into conclusions. They should also support the chain of custody by recording how evidence moved from discovery to storage or transfer.
## Report design and format
Report design should match the purpose of the document. Checklist reports suit routine tasks, inspections, daily logs and standard observations. Narrative reports suit complex incidents, undercover work, interviews and matters requiring explanation. Mixed formats often work best because checklists standardise essential data while narrative sections allow the investigator to explain context.

Some reports require the investigator to state a finding, deduction or conclusion. These sections need careful wording. The report should distinguish established facts, reasonable inferences and unresolved possibilities. When a report records an opinion, the facts supporting that opinion should appear nearby in plain language.

Digital reporting systems can improve consistency, retrieval and supervision. They do not replace judgement. A poorly completed electronic form can still omit facts, confuse chronology or over-collect personal information.

Templates should use fields that fit the investigation and the jurisdiction. They should prompt the author to record essential facts, but they should not invite unnecessary speculation or excessive personal data. They should also make continuation pages easy to attach, because complex events often exceed the space available on a standard form.

The best format is the one that makes later review reliable. A daily activity log may need little narrative. An undercover report, arrest report or serious incident report usually needs a detailed sequence of events, direct speech where relevant and clear links between observations and conclusions.
## Common report types
Private investigators and security staff use different reports for different operational needs. The main categories include:
- Activity reports, shift logs and daily logs that record time, location and work performed
- Review reports that summarise weekly, monthly or annual activity and help measure performance
- Patrol and observation reports that record surveillance points, observations and times
- Surveillance reports that document observed movement, contact and activity
- Missing person and current whereabouts reports that collect leads, identifiers and search steps
- Undercover reports that require detailed chronological narrative
- Bomb search reports that record the area searched, suspicious items, evacuation status and handover to specialist responders
- Inspection reports that identify hazards, security weaknesses and corrective action
- Security incident reports that record wrongdoing, complainants, witnesses, suspects, evidence and action taken
- Property reports that describe missing, damaged, stolen, seized or recovered property
- Traffic and vehicle accident reports that record vehicles, drivers, conditions, damage, injury, witnesses and probable causes
- Security surveys that assess physical security, lighting, locks, alarms, CCTV, fire systems, access control and general risk controls

These forms should guide observation, not replace it. A form that lacks space for a material fact should be supplemented by a continuation sheet or separate narrative. Investigators should also correct outdated or foreign fields before use in Australia. For example, social security numbers, US legal warnings and broad race fields should not be copied into Australian practice without a lawful and necessary purpose.
## Criminal documentation and legal safeguards
Major criminal documentation usually begins with an offence or general crime report. This report records the alleged conduct, suspects, victims, witnesses, evidence, property and action taken. Supplemental reports update the matter as enquiries develop.

Arrest reports require particular care. In Australia, private investigators generally do not hold the same arrest powers as police. Citizen's arrest powers vary by state and territory. Investigators should confirm the applicable law, record the lawful basis for any detention and transfer the person and any property to police as soon as required. Reports should avoid suggesting a general power to arrest on reasonable suspicion unless the relevant legislation clearly supports that position.

Interview, statement and consent forms also require local caution. US Miranda warnings are not an Australian template. Australian investigators should use jurisdiction-appropriate wording and should take special care when working with police, questioning suspected offenders, handling vulnerable people or obtaining admissions. A voluntary witness statement should identify the maker, time, place, topic, wording used, any interpreter or support person and any pressure, promise or inducement that may affect reliability.

Consent-to-search forms should record what the person authorised, what area or item the search covered, who conducted it, when consent was given and whether consent was voluntary. Investigators should not treat a signed form as a cure for coercion, overreach or unlawful collection.

Privacy also shapes report writing. Australian organisations should collect only personal information reasonably necessary for their functions, and sensitive information usually requires consent unless an exception applies. Reports should record enough detail to investigate and evidence the matter, while avoiding unnecessary personal data.
## Quality control before submission
Supervisors should treat report writing as an operational skill, not an administrative afterthought. Training, templates and review processes should reward accuracy and clarity. A firm should also require investigators to correct inconsistent names, missing dates, unexplained time gaps and unsupported conclusions before a report leaves the office.

A useful final review asks whether the report lets a reader reconstruct the matter without speaking to the author. The reviewer should be able to identify the incident, the people involved, the source of each important fact, the evidence collected, the action taken and the next required step. If the report cannot answer those questions, it needs revision.

Reports should also preserve the difference between client communication and evidentiary recordkeeping. A progress report may reassure a client, but it should not overstate findings. A final report may express a conclusion, but it should connect that conclusion to observable facts and documented enquiries.
## Practical principles
Strong investigative reports apply a few practical rules:
- Write soon after the event
- Put events in time order
- Use active voice and plain Australian English
- Record facts before inferences
- Attribute statements to the speaker
- Identify the source of information
- Record uncertainty honestly
- Keep personal data proportionate to the purpose
- Preserve continuity of evidence
- Review before submission

A professional report gives the reader a reliable path through the investigation. It allows the client to understand progress, the firm to justify its work, the investigator to refresh memory and a court or decision-maker to test the evidence. The strongest reports combine discipline, economy and completeness.
