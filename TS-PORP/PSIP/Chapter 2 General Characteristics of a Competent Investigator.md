# Chapter 2: General Characteristics of a Competent Investigator
## Purpose and professional stance
Competent investigators combine curiosity, discipline, legal awareness, practical judgement, accurate note-taking and resilience. They do not merely collect information. They decide what information matters, test it, preserve it and explain its significance in a form that other people can rely on.

A sound investigation begins with a clear understanding of role and authority. Public police and private investigators may use similar skills, including interviewing, surveillance, document review and evidence handling. Their powers and purposes differ. Police investigate crime for public purposes, including arrest, prosecution, public safety and prevention. Private investigators and private security practitioners usually act for clients or organisations. They may investigate internal misconduct, insurance claims, employment issues, due diligence, asset protection, missing persons, background matters, workplace incidents, civil disputes or evidence relevant to litigation.

Private practitioners can support the justice system by finding reliable facts, locating people or property, identifying leads, testing claims and preserving material that may later assist a court, insurer, employer or regulator. They do not become police because their work overlaps with policing. They must act within their licence, the law, contractual authority, privacy obligations and the limits of any client instruction.
## Objectivity
Objectivity gives an investigation its value. An investigator who starts with a fixed conclusion risks selecting only the facts that confirm it. A competent investigator starts with questions, not answers. They separate evidence from assumption, distinguish firsthand accounts from hearsay and record uncertainty when the available information does not justify a firm conclusion.

Objective practice requires emotional control. Investigations often arise from conflict, fear, embarrassment, injury, financial loss or public controversy. A good investigator recognises those pressures without absorbing them. They avoid anger, sympathy, loyalty to the client and personal prejudice as substitutes for evidence. They also recognise that stress can impair decision-making, especially during urgent or confrontational incidents.

Training supports objectivity because it gives investigators a repeatable method when circumstances become difficult. Structured notes, checklists, lawful evidence handling, clear reporting templates and planned interviews reduce the risk of omission. The aim is not mechanical procedure for its own sake. The aim is reliable reconstruction of what happened, what did not happen and what remains unknown.
## Logic and inquiry
Logical investigation uses orderly reasoning to connect facts, causes, people, places and times. It asks the basic questions in a disciplined way:
- Who was involved, affected, present, absent or responsible?
- What happened, what evidence exists and what rules or legal elements matter?
- Where did the relevant events occur and where might further evidence be found?
- When did each event occur, who knew about it and when did they know?
- How did the event happen, how was access gained and how was evidence created or moved?
- Why might the event have occurred and why do particular accounts differ?

These questions help investigators build a chronology, identify gaps, test cause and effect and avoid premature conclusions. They also help keep reports clear. A report should show the reader the route from evidence to conclusion. It should not hide weak links behind confident language.

A theory of the case remains provisional. Investigators may form hypotheses, but they must revise or abandon them when facts contradict them. Good reasoning compares competing explanations. It asks whether another person, cause, motive, location or sequence better fits the evidence. It also recognises that a plausible story is not necessarily a proven one.

The same discipline applies to interviews. Investigators should not treat demeanour as proof of truth or deception. Nervousness, hesitation, eye contact, posture and tone can reflect fear, trauma, culture, disability, language difficulty or the pressure of being questioned. Interviewers should prefer open questions, careful listening, accurate summaries and later verification against independent evidence. Apparent inconsistency should prompt further inquiry, not instant accusation.
## Perseverance and judgement
Investigations rarely move in a straight line. They involve missing records, reluctant witnesses, false leads, incomplete memories, poor systems and long periods of routine work. Surveillance, audit, inventory checks, title searches, tax searches, file reviews and background inquiries may appear dull, yet they often produce the decisive detail.

Perseverance matters because important evidence often appears only after repeated, lawful and methodical effort. Unknown witnesses, overlooked documents and small inconsistencies seldom emerge by chance. They emerge because an investigator keeps following leads after the easy inquiries have ended.

Perseverance is not stubbornness. A determined investigator still changes direction when the evidence requires it. They do not continue a weak theory to save face. They do not accept a client allegation merely because the client pays the fee. They examine facts favourable and unfavourable to the client, because a one-sided investigation lacks credibility and may fail when tested.

Professional patience also protects fairness. In family, workplace, fraud, insurance and liability matters, allegations can damage reputations before any finding is made. A competent investigator treats all parties with procedural fairness, keeps records of sources and methods and avoids language that states more than the evidence proves.
## Human relations and communication
Investigation is people-centred work. Even document-heavy matters depend on people who create, store, explain, conceal, lose or interpret records. Investigators therefore need strong interpersonal skills. They must speak clearly, listen closely, build rapport, adapt to different personalities and remain calm with hostile, distressed or evasive people.

Respect improves evidence quality. Witnesses provide fuller and more accurate information when they understand the purpose of the conversation and feel safe enough to speak. Rapport does not require manipulation or false friendship. It requires courtesy, attention, patience, plain language and control of the interview environment.

Good communication includes nonverbal awareness, but nonverbal signals should be treated carefully. Clothing, posture, facial expression, hygiene, tone, word choice and written style all affect the impression an investigator creates. The investigator should appear professional, neutral and organised. They should also avoid reading too much into another person's body language.

Effective investigators manage conversations without dominating them. They ask broad questions first, allow uninterrupted accounts, then use focused questions to clarify details. They avoid compound questions, leading questions and jargon. They check understanding, note exact words when precision matters and distinguish what a person saw from what they inferred.

Clear written communication is equally important. The best investigation can lose force if the report is vague, disorganised or inflated. Reports should identify the task, scope, sources, relevant facts, limitations, analysis and conclusion. They should use direct language and preserve important caveats.
## Professional traits
A competent investigator needs a cluster of habits rather than a single personality type. The most useful traits include:
- Alertness and careful observation
- Honesty and reliability
- Calm judgement under pressure
- Impartiality and fairness
- Practical intelligence and resourcefulness
- Patience and persistence
- Respect for law and procedure
- Clear memory supported by accurate notes
- Thoroughness in small details
- Flexibility when facts change

These traits reinforce each other. Observation without judgement produces noise. Persistence without objectivity becomes fixation. Intelligence without ethics becomes a risk. Communication without accuracy becomes persuasion rather than investigation.
## Ethics and professional standards
Ethics turn investigative skill into legitimate practice. Investigators often handle sensitive information, vulnerable people, contested allegations and material that can affect employment, liberty, reputation, money and family relationships. They must therefore act with honesty, legality, confidentiality and restraint.

Ethical practice requires investigators to avoid falsifying records, misusing client property, disclosing confidential information, accepting improper benefits, misleading clients, using unlawful surveillance, making false claims about qualifications or conducting work beyond competence. It also requires them to disclose conflicts of interest, charge reasonable and agreed fees and refuse instructions that require unlawful or abusive conduct.

Professional codes are useful because they translate broad values into daily expectations. Security and investigation bodies commonly emphasise lawful conduct, truthfulness, competence, diligence, confidentiality, respect for rights and avoidance of conduct that harms clients, colleagues, employers or the public. These standards are not decorative. They guide decisions when the client wants a shortcut, when a witness is vulnerable, when evidence is tempting but unlawfully obtained or when a practitioner is asked to exceed their expertise.

Licensing and training also matter. In Australia, private investigation and security work is regulated primarily through state and territory systems. Requirements differ, but licensing commonly addresses age, identity, character, training and fit-and-proper-person standards. In New South Wales, for example, a Class 2E licence authorises private investigation work and current applicants must complete the Certificate III in Investigative Services. Such requirements help set a minimum threshold, but they do not replace continuing professional development, supervision and quality assurance.

Self-assessment is not enough. Investigators may overrate their interviewing skill, judgement or objectivity. Agencies and employers should review reports, audit files, test compliance, maintain training and correct poor practice early. Low pay, poor training, weak supervision and high turnover undermine ethical standards because they encourage rushed work, unsuitable recruitment and a culture of minimum effort.

Public trust remains essential. Investigators need access to people, premises, records and institutions. They lose that access when the occupation appears careless, intrusive or dishonest. The competent investigator protects that trust by producing work that is lawful, fair, well-reasoned and useful.
