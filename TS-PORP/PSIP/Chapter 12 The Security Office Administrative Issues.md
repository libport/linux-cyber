# Chapter 12: The Security Office: Administrative Issues
## Administrative discipline
A security office depends on disciplined administration as much as investigative skill. Each matter generates instructions, appointments, evidence, reports, expenses, invoices and employee records. Without a controlled system, staff waste time, miss deadlines, lose information and weaken the organisation's position with clients, regulators and courts.

Effective administration treats the security operation as an integrated business system. Applicant tracking, onboarding, rostering, operations, payroll, invoicing, accounting and reporting must connect. The system should show who accepted the work, what the client requested, which tasks staff completed, what evidence exists, what remains outstanding and what costs have accrued.

Poor administration damages profit and trust. Common time-wasters include unclear priorities, unnecessary meetings, duplicate work, weak delegation, excessive communication, poor filing, interruptions, unfinished tasks and slow decisions. Managers reduce those losses by setting priorities, using standard forms, assigning responsibility, checking progress and removing avoidable distractions.
## Timekeeping and scheduling
Security work runs on time, cost and availability. Investigators and supervisors should record appointments, field assignments, deadlines, travel, surveillance hours, report writing and client contact in a shared scheduling system. A reliable calendar prevents double-booking and helps the office allocate staff to the right task at the right time.

Appointments should match the likely complexity of the matter. A first conference or complex file requires enough time to understand the facts, risks and resources. Routine matters can move faster, especially where support staff can gather preliminary information. Reminder calls, emails or messages help clients attend appointments and preserve goodwill when a meeting is missed.
## Documents and reports
Administrative documents turn raw information into a usable record. Intake forms, interview reports, conference notes, investigation requests, daily activity reports and expense summaries give each file structure. They help later readers understand the matter without relying on memory.

Reports should be concise, factual and complete enough for the client to act. Investigators should remove repetition, avoid vague language and record facts in plain terms. The purpose is not to produce the longest report. The purpose is to capture the relevant facts, methods, dates, sources, limits and next steps.

Good documentation also protects the firm. Clear notes, correspondence and signed instructions can answer later allegations of negligence, delay, overcharging or poor performance. Incident and injury reports should record what happened, where it happened, who was involved, what task the employee was performing and what follow-up occurred.
## Software and information control
Modern security offices usually need software for case management, rostering, incident reporting, document storage, billing and communication. A useful system should:
- Generate standard letters, authorities and reports.
- Index documents, evidence, witnesses, events and tasks.
- Search across files quickly.
- Support mobile reporting and secure file access.
- Track time, expenses, invoices and payments.
- Archive completed matters for later reference.
- Allow controlled access based on role and need.

The office should choose software for reliability, usability, interoperability and privacy controls rather than novelty. Systems that store personal information must protect it from misuse, loss, unauthorised access, modification and disclosure. Information that no longer needs to be retained should be destroyed or de-identified when the law permits.
## Filing and reminders
A filing system should be centralised, consistent and easy to audit. One responsible person or role should control file creation, naming, storage, removal, return and disposal. Useful file categories include current matters, correspondence, billing, invoices, internal memoranda, research, phone notes, work papers, photographs, video, investigation reports and witness statements.

Numerical or colour-coded systems can identify the client, year opened, annual file number and case type. A standard code helps staff locate files and allows another investigator to understand a matter quickly. Documents should be clipped or secured, with the newest material placed consistently according to office policy.

A tickler system tracks future action. It may use software, a shared calendar, index cards or a diary. The system should capture appointments, interviews, report dates, billing dates, limitation periods, follow-up tasks and file closure dates. Staff should review it daily.
## Billing and financial control
Billing requires accuracy and speed. Clients are more likely to question invoices when the office delays billing or cannot explain the work. Investigators should record billable time, non-billable time, travel, mileage, meals, accommodation, parking, tolls, phone costs, cash advances and credit card purchases as they occur.

The firm should set fees under written policy and confirm client authority before work begins. Where payment risk exists, a retainer protects the firm. A retainer or travel advance remains company money until earned or reconciled. It is not wages.

Payroll, rostering, timekeeping and billing are connected. In Australia, employers must keep time and wage records in English, make them accessible and retain them for seven years. Accurate records support correct pay, correct invoicing and defensible management.
## Risk, safety and accountability
Administration supports the duty to keep workers and others safe. A person conducting a business or undertaking must, so far as reasonably practicable, provide safe work systems, information, training, supervision and a safe work environment. Those duties include physical and psychological health.

A well-run security office reduces legal exposure by showing what work was authorised, what risks were considered, what staff did, what the client received and how the organisation handled money and records. Administrative control therefore strengthens service quality, profitability, compliance and professional credibility.
