# Chapter 10: Investigative Method and Technique Insurance Cases
## Insurance investigation
Insurance work gives private investigators a substantial role because fraud, disputed loss and large claim volumes create a constant need for independent fact-finding. Insurers may use staff investigators, but they often retain external investigators for commercial, casualty, property, motor vehicle and workers compensation matters. Catastrophes and high-volume claims increase that reliance.

Insurance fraud can arise before, during or after a policy takes effect. It may involve applicants, policyholders, third-party claimants, brokers, agents, service providers or professionals who support a claim. Common schemes affect underwriting, property claims, motor vehicles, life insurance, liability insurance, health insurance and workers compensation.

The investigator's task is not to decide liability or settle the claim. The task is to gather reliable facts so the insurer, insured, lawyers or decision makers can assess coverage, credibility, quantum and legal exposure. Effective insurance investigation usually answers these questions:
- Did the claimed event occur?
- Does the policy respond to the event?
- Who was involved and what did each person do?
- What caused the loss?
- What injury, damage or illness resulted?
- Does the claimant's history support or weaken the claim?
- Are witnesses credible and consistent?
- Does lawful surveillance confirm or contradict the claimed limitations?
- Are recoverable assets, subrogation rights or other remedies available?

Insurance investigations commonly cover death, property damage, personal injury, bodily injury, illness and disability. Each case requires the same disciplined method: obtain facts, preserve evidence, record sources, test competing explanations and report objectively.
## Core method
A competent insurance investigation starts with the policy, the claim file and the event chronology. The investigator identifies the parties, the insured property or person, the policy period, the type of coverage, exclusions, limits, notice requirements and any conditions precedent to payment. The investigator then builds a factual record through interviews, document review, scene inspection, searches, medical or technical material, photographs, video and expert input where needed.

The method must fit the claim type. A hail damage claim needs different expertise from a suspected arson. A suicide investigation differs from a fatal motor vehicle collision. A disability claim needs different proof from a stolen vehicle claim. Investigators should therefore use claim-specific checklists, but they must not let forms replace judgement.

Lawful practice is central. Drones, GPS devices, remote surveillance, database searches and neighbourhood inquiries may assist, but each technique must comply with privacy law, surveillance devices law, licensing rules, evidence rules, client authority and professional ethics. Investigators should also avoid collecting irrelevant personal information, especially where it could produce bias or discriminatory reasoning.

Investigators should also match the scope of work to the claim's value and risk. A minor property claim may need a brief inspection, photographs and a short report. A suspected organised fraud, fatal crash or major fire may need multiple interviews, expert analysis, laboratory testing, financial inquiries and legal oversight. Proportionality protects clients from unnecessary cost and protects investigators from overreaching.

The first review should identify urgent steps. Evidence may disappear quickly after a fire, crash, theft or injury. Vehicles may be repaired, scenes cleaned, witnesses become harder to locate and electronic records may be overwritten. Early action should secure documents, preserve physical evidence, identify witnesses and record the scene before normal business processes destroy useful detail.
## Fire and arson claims
Property fire claims require the investigator to determine whether the loss falls within the policy and whether the fire was accidental, natural, negligent or deliberately lit. If investigators exclude arson, coverage often turns on valuation, deductibles, policy terms and proof of damage.

Arson is harder. Fire investigation requires scientific method, scene control and specialist expertise. Burn patterns, ventilation, fuel load, accelerants, electrical sources and suppression activity can mislead untrained observers. A deliberate fire should not be inferred from suspicion, financial pressure or unusual behaviour alone. The conclusion requires credible physical evidence, reliable witness evidence and defensible expert analysis.

The investigator should establish motive without treating motive as proof. Relevant factors may include recent insurance increases, financial distress, lease pressure, poor business performance, relocation pressure, neighbourhood crime, removed contents, earlier fires, threats, revenge, vandalism or concealment of another crime. These factors guide inquiry, but the physical origin and cause remain critical.

Witness work matters because fire scenes are often occupied, visible from nearby premises or attended by many responders. Investigators should identify tenants, neighbours, passers-by, business owners, customers, firefighters, police, emergency medical staff, utilities personnel, insurance brokers, adjusters and anyone who saw the scene before, during or after the fire. Interviews should document times, smoke, flames, odours, alarms, access, locked or open doors, sprinkler performance, removed property, vehicles, suspicious persons and any statements made by the insured.

Scene work should proceed from safety and authority. The investigator must identify the incident commander, confirm legal access, assess hazards, define the perimeter, protect evidence, record first-responder observations and document any changes made during suppression. Where origin or cause is unclear, or where an incendiary cause appears possible, a qualified fire investigator should conduct or direct the examination.
## Workers compensation and disability claims
Workers compensation replaces ordinary negligence litigation with a statutory compensation process in many workplace injury matters. Fault usually matters less than whether the injury arose out of, or in the course of, employment. The rules vary by jurisdiction, and disputed claims can still enter formal review, tribunal or court processes.

The investigator should verify the existence, nature and work connection of the injury. Useful information includes the date, time and place of the incident, job duties, witnesses, notice to the employer, medical treatment, hospitalisation, prior injuries, wages, time lost, return-to-work status, third-party liability and any recorded statements. A good investigation also distinguishes exaggeration, misunderstanding, delayed diagnosis and genuine injury.

Surveillance can assist when a claimant's alleged limitations conflict with observed activity, but it must be proportionate, lawful and relevant. The investigator should not seek dramatic footage for its own sake. The useful question is whether the recorded activity bears on the claimed injury. Lifting, bending, carrying, running, climbing, driving, working, twisting, crouching or using tools may matter when those movements are medically relevant.

Disability claims require similar discipline. Investigators should confirm hospitalisation, treating doctors, mobility aids, restrictions, work status, time lost, previous accidents, medical history, driving and daily activities. On-site interviews and neighbourhood inquiries can help, but pretext approaches create legal and ethical risk. Investigators should not misrepresent identity or purpose unless the method is lawful, authorised and necessary. Inquiries should focus on observable activity and claim-relevant facts, not gossip, protected attributes or character judgements.

Final reports should separate observation from inference. They should record dates, times, locations, vehicles, weather, visibility, camera use, interviews, documents reviewed and the reasons surveillance began and ended. A defensible report explains what the subject did, why it matters to the claim and what limitations remain in the evidence.
## Motor vehicle liability claims
Motor vehicle claims begin with the insurance contract. The declaration page identifies coverage such as bodily injury liability, property damage liability, collision, comprehensive cover, medical payments, towing, funeral expenses and uninsured or underinsured motorist cover. The policy then sets out coverages, exclusions, limits and conditions.

Investigators provide facts for claim strategy, not legal rulings. A motor vehicle crash may involve negligence, first-party benefits, intentional torts, statutory compensation, uninsured motorist cover, underinsured motorist cover or overlapping insurance obligations. The investigator should identify what happened, who was involved, what damage occurred and what evidence supports each account.

A crash investigation seeks the chain of events. Key points include the principal event, perception of danger, point of perception, point of no escape, maximum engagement and final resting positions. Unless safety or emergency response requires movement, vehicles, bodies, debris and damaged objects should remain undisturbed until properly documented.

Core records include police reports, witness statements, medical summaries, repair estimates, photographs, vehicle data, licence and registration details, insurance details, road conditions and scene measurements. Clients should be asked to sketch the accident from memory, but investigators should test that sketch against physical evidence and independent accounts.
## Photography and reconstruction
Photographs are essential in crash work. They should show the approach to the scene from each driver's viewpoint, the viewpoint of witnesses, final vehicle positions, victim positions, point of impact, overall scene layout and close details. Close details include skid marks, tyre marks, gouges, debris, broken parts, fluid spills, number plates, lamps, tyres, seatbelts, load condition and vehicle interiors where relevant.

Good photographic practice records distance, angle, focal point and context. A close-up without scale may prove little. A panoramic image without detail may miss the decisive fact. Investigators should use photographs to connect the roadway, vehicles, marks, witnesses and claimed movements.

Accident reconstruction has levels. Basic reporting identifies people, property, time, place, weather, damage and witnesses. Supplementary data collection adds measurements, photographs, statements, vehicle damage, biological samples where lawful, paint, glass and medical information. Technical preparation maps the scene, estimates speed from marks, matches damage and prepares diagrams. Professional reconstruction explains positions, visibility, evasive action, direction, speed and injury mechanisms. Cause analysis then evaluates probable contributing factors.

Reconstruction requires competence. Investigators should recognise when engineers, crash reconstruction specialists, vehicle examiners, medical experts or other specialists must be engaged. Experience alone does not justify unsupported conclusions.
## Vehicle theft, fraud and parts claims
Vehicle theft, staged loss, false reporting and parts fraud remain major insurance issues. The National Insurance Crime Bureau, not the National Crime Insurance Bureau, developed from earlier insurance crime bodies and provides resources such as VINCheck in the United States.

Common fraudulent vehicle loss scenarios include false theft reports, owner-arranged disposal, export fraud, inflated value, purchase-and-report schemes, arson after financial pressure, staged recovery and strip fraud. Warning signs include recent ownership, private or interstate purchase, cash payment, missing or duplicate title, inability to locate a prior owner, a new policy followed quickly by a loss, late notice, no police report, unusual recovery conditions, heavy collision damage or a fire in an isolated location at night.

Vehicle identification is central. Investigators should understand registration documents, title documents, insurance identifiers, manufacturer plates, engine numbers, transmission numbers and Vehicle Identification Numbers. In the United States, modern vehicles use a 17-character VIN under federal standards. VINs and derivative part numbers can connect a vehicle, part or suspect to a specific loss.

Investigators should inspect common VIN locations, check for tampering, compare numbers across documents and vehicle components, and seek expert help where stamping, labels or electronic data appear altered. Manufacturers may also mark major parts such as engines, transmissions, doors, guards and body panels. Counterfeit, removed or inconsistent marks can indicate theft, rebirthing or parts trafficking.

Professional development matters in this area. Organisations such as the International Association of Auto Theft Investigators (IAATI) support training and technical knowledge.
## Documentation and claim control
Documentation gives the investigation its value. Each file should show who requested the work, what question the investigator was asked to answer, what authority supported the inquiry, what sources were checked and what limits affected the result. Claim files should preserve notes, interview records, photographs, video logs, database results, correspondence, medical authorisations, chain-of-custody records and expert instructions.

Investigators should record negative findings as well as positive findings. A witness who saw nothing, a camera that did not capture the event, a VIN location that showed no alteration or a surveillance period with no activity may still matter. These details prevent later readers from assuming that obvious inquiries were ignored.

Statements require special care. The investigator should capture the witness's own words where precision matters, confirm times and locations, separate direct observation from assumption and avoid leading compound questions. Recorded or written statements should identify the interviewer, interviewee, date, place, start and finish time, people present and any documents or images shown during the interview.

Physical evidence needs continuity. Fire debris, vehicle parts, documents, photographs, electronic data and samples should be collected only by authorised people using suitable containers and labels. The file should show where each item came from, who handled it, how it was stored and when it was transferred. Poor continuity can damage an otherwise strong claim investigation.
## Professional standards
Insurance investigation affects money, reputation, employment, liberty, safety and privacy. Investigators must therefore act with accuracy, restraint and independence. They should avoid speculation, exaggeration, unlawful surveillance, irrelevant personal data, unsupported arson findings, biased assumptions about injury and conclusions that belong to lawyers, courts, tribunals or insurers.

The best reports are factual, clear, chronological and complete enough to be tested. They identify sources, show how evidence was obtained, distinguish observation from opinion, preserve uncertainty and recommend specialist review when needed. Abridged forms and checklists are useful only when they support, rather than replace, careful reasoning.
