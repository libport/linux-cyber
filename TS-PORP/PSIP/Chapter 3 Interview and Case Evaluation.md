# Interview and case evaluation
## Purpose of investigative interviews
Investigative interviewing turns initial information into reliable leads, tested evidence and practical case decisions. A competent investigator does not treat interviewing as casual conversation or as an assumed security skill. The investigator plans the interview, sets a respectful tone, asks purposeful questions and evaluates the answers against the case theory and available evidence.

Each interview can identify new witnesses, documents, records or inconsistencies. The investigator follows credible leads with background research, contact with the new source and a further interview where appropriate. Case development remains iterative. One source can expose another, and a lead can strengthen, redirect or weaken an investigation.

The interviewer's aim is to gather accurate information, clarify issues and preserve a professional relationship with the interviewee. Rapport supports accuracy, but rapport must not become manipulation. Ethical interviewing uses active listening, open questions, clear explanations and careful follow-up. Coercion, false authority, intimidation and pressure tactics can contaminate information and create legal risk.
## Planning and first contact
The initial interview shapes the case and the relationship. It gives the investigator an opportunity to confirm the client's purpose, identify the parties, understand the alleged events and decide whether the assignment is lawful, safe and worthwhile.

Effective initial interviews require:
- a convenient and private time and place
- a clear plan for topics, objectives and documents
- respectful treatment of every interviewee
- questions that distinguish fact, opinion and assumption
- accurate written records of what the interviewee states
- regular communication about missing information and next steps

The investigator should not patronise interviewees, dominate the setting or rely on status to obtain answers. Interviewers may consider gender, culture, age, trauma, disability, language and subject expertise when assigning staff, but no category guarantees better results. The appropriate interviewer is the person most likely to obtain accurate information lawfully, safely and respectfully.
## Questioning method
The investigator should begin with broad, open prompts that allow the interviewee to give an account in the interviewee's own words. The investigator should then clarify times, places, people, documents and observable conduct. Closed questions can confirm precise details, but leading questions should be used sparingly because they can distort memory and weaken later evidence.

Interview notes should separate what the interviewee saw, heard or did from what the interviewee believes, inferred or was told by others. The investigator should record uncertainty, contradictions and gaps rather than smoothing them into a false narrative. Pauses, silence and repetition may help an interviewee think, but they should not be used to create pressure or suggest that the investigator already knows the answer.
## Authority, records and privacy
Information gathering requires legal authority, proper consent or another lawful basis. A release form can authorise access to employment, education, medical, credit or law enforcement records, but the investigator must request only information that is relevant, necessary and permitted. Personal information requires careful handling, secure storage, limited disclosure and correction when inaccurate.

Written instruments give structure to the interview and protect the file from memory gaps. Useful tools include request-for-investigation forms, authority-to-release forms, fact sheets, checklists, client contact reports and follow-up letters. These tools should record the client's details, the subject's details, the investigation type, budget, contact preferences, known risks, legal proceedings, known witnesses, documents sought and evidence already available.

Forms should support judgement, not replace it. The investigator still needs to probe unclear answers, test assumptions, identify contradictions and update the plan as new information emerges.

File records should also show why information was sought, how it was obtained and who received it. This audit trail protects privileged material, supports evidentiary continuity and helps supervisors detect overcollection. Sensitive identifiers, medical records, financial records and children's information require stricter access controls and should not be copied into working notes unless they advance a defined investigative purpose.
## Case-specific information
Personal injury and insurance matters require early attention to the incident, injury, medical treatment, employment consequences, lost income, prior claims, pre-existing conditions, witnesses, photographs, reports and insurance details. Immediate injuries and permanent injuries should be recorded separately because they affect causation, treatment, damages and future loss.

Product liability enquiries focus on the product, ownership, access, storage, photographs, warnings, instructions, maintenance, prior defects, manner of use and possible misuse. These questions help test whether the product was defective, whether the user followed instructions and whether another cause explains the loss.

Family law and domestic matters require special care. Questionnaires can record identities, addresses, marriage details, separation dates, children, maintenance, employment and grounds relevant to the dispute. Surveillance, vehicle tracking, device imaging and access to smartphones or home computers must be treated as high-risk techniques. A private investigator is not exempt from privacy, surveillance, stalking, trespass, evidence and computer access laws. Digital evidence should be acquired only with consent, lawful authority or proper forensic instructions.
## Follow-up communication
Follow-up letters help clients and witnesses understand what has been discussed and what action will follow. In injury matters, a letter may ask the client to preserve receipts, provide medical bills, report new treatment, identify new witnesses and redirect insurer enquiries to the representative. The letter should not encourage secrecy, falsehood or interference with another party's legitimate rights.

Client contact reports help firms monitor service quality and maintain continuity. They should record the date, method of contact, participants, matters discussed and actions required. Clear records improve supervision, reduce duplication and support later case evaluation.
## Evaluation and later interviews
After information is collected, the investigator evaluates reliability, relevance and sufficiency. Case evaluation should compare the account with documents, physical evidence, digital evidence, witness statements, timelines, motive, opportunity and known inconsistencies.

Client screening is necessary. Investigators should be cautious where a proposed assignment appears motivated by revenge, harassment, unlawful surveillance, unrealistic financial expectations, concealed conflicts or pressure to mislead. Social change objectives or strong emotions do not automatically make a client unreliable. Each case must be assessed on its facts.

Witnesses may be friendly, reluctant, hostile, adverse or expert. Reluctance may arise from fear, loyalty, trauma, confusion, cultural concerns, disability, legal exposure or anxiety about court. The investigator should identify the reason before judging credibility. Timely interviews, factual questions, written statements and ongoing contact preserve information and support later proceedings.
## Professional and ethical controls
Investigators should avoid pretext interviews unless a lawyer or other responsible authority has confirmed that the tactic is lawful and ethical in the relevant jurisdiction. They must not impersonate police, government officials, lawyers or other regulated professionals. They must not misrepresent their authority, trespass, access private accounts, intercept communications or obtain records by unlawful means.

Sound interviewing rests on preparation, respect, accurate recording and lawful evidence handling. Professional standards protect the client, the investigator, the interviewee and the integrity of the case.
