# Chapter 7:  Surveillance Techniques
## Purpose and lawful scope
Surveillance means systematic observation by personal observation, technology, or both. Security investigators use it to gather information, protect people, test information, identify risks and produce evidence. Covert surveillance can support investigations, but visible surveillance also has value because CCTV signs and cameras can deter misconduct and encourage safer behaviour.

Effective surveillance starts with a lawful and proportionate purpose. It is not an unlimited investigative tool. Law, privacy, trespass, workplace rules, contracts, data protection duties and evidence rules set boundaries. In Australia, surveillance that captures identifiable people can involve personal information, so organisations must collect only what they need, explain collection where required, secure recordings and delete or de-identify material when it no longer serves a lawful purpose. State and territory laws also regulate listening devices, optical devices, tracking devices and workplace surveillance.

Private investigators do not gain police powers by conducting surveillance. They must not enter private property without authority, impersonate public officials, obtain restricted records by deception, install trackers without lawful authority, record private conversations unlawfully, or use equipment that intrudes into homes, bathrooms, change rooms or other spaces where people reasonably expect privacy. Evidence gathered through unlawful or oppressive methods may damage a case and expose the investigator, employer or client to liability.
## Main functions
Surveillance may serve several legitimate functions:
- Protect an undercover operative and corroborate that person's account.
- Obtain evidence of suspected crime, civil wrongdoing, fraud or workplace misconduct.
- Locate a person, property, contraband or stolen goods.
- Test the reliability of informants, witnesses and other sources.
- Develop leads from other intelligence.
- Observe post-transaction movement of money, drugs, stolen property or other goods.
- Support police, lawyers or regulators with admissible evidence when the investigator has gathered it lawfully.

Investigators often classify surveillance by function. Intelligence surveillance collects information about a person, place, vehicle, business or activity. Preparatory surveillance supports a later enforcement action or internal intervention. Protective surveillance backs an undercover operative. Post-transaction surveillance traces goods, money and contacts after a sale, delivery or transfer.

Digital surveillance now sits beside physical surveillance. CCTV, access logs, network activity, location data, databases and open-source information can reveal patterns that physical observation may miss. These tools also create data governance duties, because greater technical capacity increases the risk of over-collection, misuse and unjustified intrusion.
## Planning and control
Good surveillance depends on planning, organising, directing and controlling the work. The plan must identify the objective, the subject, the legal basis, the location, the timing, the personnel, the budget, the equipment, the communication method and the exit conditions.

Before deployment, the investigator should answer several questions:
- Does a less intrusive method exist?
- What information does the client need?
- What conduct, location or event must the team document?
- Which form of surveillance best fits the task?
- What laws apply in the jurisdiction?
- What privacy risks require controls?
- What equipment, vehicles and personnel does the operation need?
- How will the team record observations, preserve evidence and maintain chain of custody?
- What circumstances require the team to stop?

The objective must be specific. Surveillance should not operate as an open-ended search for anything suspicious. A disability investigation may focus on activities inconsistent with a claimed injury. A retail theft investigation may focus on a specific exit point, register or employee. A corporate fraud investigation may focus on meetings, transfers, devices, documents or movements that connect suspects to a loss. Objectives can change when new information appears, but the supervisor should approve material changes and record the reason.
## Location, timing and subject
Investigators must know the geography before they start. They should study maps, roads, public transport, parking, lighting, entry points, exits, sight lines, cover positions, local dress and neighbourhood routines. They should identify police stations, hospitals, high-risk areas and safe withdrawal routes. The best surveillance position allows observation without trespass, obstruction or obvious attention.

Timing affects risk and quality. Daylight may improve image quality, while night may reduce detection. Rush hour can help a team blend into traffic but can also break contact with the subject. Poor lighting, glare, weather and distance can reduce the value of photos or video. The duration should match the objective, the budget and the likely pattern of activity.

A subject may be a person, vehicle, premises, business, group, object or digital account. The type of subject affects the permissible level of intrusion. A person selling drugs in a public street has a lower expectation of privacy than a person inside a home. Surveillance of a residence requires the greatest restraint, especially when investigators use long lenses, zoom, drones, tracking devices or hidden cameras. The team should document only matters relevant to the objective and avoid recording embarrassing, intimate or unrelated activity.
## Personnel and conduct
Surveillance demands patience, concentration, memory, flexibility and calm judgement. Investigators must blend into the environment. Clothing, vehicles and behaviour should look ordinary for the location. Bright vehicles, unusual accessories, visible antennas, loud exhausts, conspicuous cameras and unnatural movements increase the risk of detection.

Undercover and covert work require stricter control. Operators should not form sexual or emotional relationships with subjects, consume alcohol during an assignment, steal property, encourage offences, handle contraband without authority, or carry identity documents that reveal their role. They should remove identifying notes and equipment from vehicles. Firearms, restraints and similar equipment should never appear on a checklist unless the person has lawful authority, training and an operational need.

Investigators must also manage the risk of confrontation. If a subject suspects surveillance, the investigator should act naturally, disengage safely, avoid argument and report the compromise. Continuing after a clear compromise can endanger the team and weaken the evidence.
## Equipment and evidence quality
Equipment must suit the objective. The planner should assess cameras, lenses, batteries, memory cards, radios, phones, chargers, binoculars, vehicle choice, maps, forms, personal safety equipment and secure evidence storage. Equipment checks matter because technical failure can waste the operation or compromise safety.

CCTV can support deterrence, detection and response, but it does not always justify its cost. A sound CCTV plan starts with a needs analysis:
- Which areas require coverage?
- Which assets, people or processes need protection?
- Where are the main vulnerabilities?
- Can the network, storage and monitoring system support the cameras?
- Will CCTV integrate with access control, alarms and incident response?
- Does the budget cover training, maintenance, upgrades and secure retention?

CCTV has limits. Screens may miss small details, multiple feeds can divide attention, long monitoring causes fatigue, obstructions can block coverage and cameras cannot intervene by themselves. CCTV works best when trained staff review footage, respond quickly and maintain the system.

Photography and video must clearly identify the subject and the relevant action. Digital cameras improve capture and storage, but they do not remove the need for appropriate lenses, stable positioning, correct exposure, adequate light, verified timestamps and careful notes. Long-distance identification still depends on optics, angle and image quality. Unclear images invite mistaken identity arguments and may have little evidentiary value.

Motion-triggered devices, photoelectric sensors, vibration sensors, audio triggers and other automated systems can start recordings when activity occurs. Investigators must ensure that any trigger, recording and storage method complies with surveillance device and privacy law.

Evidence handling should preserve integrity from capture to report. Investigators should record the camera details, file names, date, time, location, vantage point, distance, subject description, activity observed and storage method. They should keep original files secure, restrict access, avoid unnecessary editing and document every transfer.
## Stationary surveillance
Stationary surveillance places an investigator or device in a fixed position to observe a subject, site or event. It may use a room, parked vehicle, public vantage point or other lawful cover. It often involves long periods without activity, so fatigue and boredom pose real risks. Supervisors should set shift lengths, communication checks and relief arrangements.

The investigator should not abandon a sound plan too early because nothing happens. Lack of activity does not prove compromise. The team should reassess only against objective signs, such as detection, safety risks, legal concerns, equipment failure, budget limits or completion of the objective.
## Foot surveillance
Foot surveillance can involve one investigator or a team. Single-person surveillance costs less but creates a higher risk of losing the subject or being recognised. Two-person teams allow one investigator to maintain contact while the other watches for associates, changes position or withdraws if noticed.

Three-person foot surveillance, often called the ABC method, offers greater flexibility. One investigator follows behind the subject, a second follows the first, and a third uses the opposite side of the street or another supporting position. In crowded areas, all three may stay on the same side, with each person tracking the next position. The aim is to maintain contact without creating a visible pattern.

Subjects who suspect surveillance may reverse direction, watch reflections, drop objects, board and leave transport at the last moment, pause around corners, use multi-exit buildings, change appearance or use a decoy. The team must anticipate these behaviours and rotate positions naturally.
## Vehicle surveillance
Vehicle surveillance tracks a subject's vehicle or supports observation of premises. Two people in a surveillance vehicle usually produce better results because the driver must focus on safe driving while the second investigator observes, navigates, communicates and prepares to continue on foot.

Investigators should obey traffic laws, drive safely and cooperate with police if stopped. Copying illegal turns, running red lights or tailgating can reveal the operation and create serious risk. Distance depends on traffic, road layout, speed, visibility and the subject's awareness. Multiple vehicles reduce detection because they can rotate the lead position and avoid presenting the same vehicle in the subject's mirrors.

Common vehicle tactics include progressive surveillance, leading surveillance and parallel surveillance. Progressive surveillance places investigators at points along an expected route rather than maintaining a continuous tail. Leading surveillance keeps a known subject between vehicles when the route is predictable. Parallel surveillance uses nearby streets to maintain direction without following directly. Each tactic requires communication discipline and a clear decision point for disengagement.
## Hybrid methods, public transport and pretext
Hybrid surveillance combines vehicles and foot teams. It suits urban environments where a subject may park, walk, enter public transport or move through multi-exit buildings. The passenger in a vehicle can become the foot investigator when the subject leaves a car or when traffic prevents the vehicle from maintaining sight.

Public transport surveillance requires careful positioning. The investigator should board close enough to keep contact, sit behind and on the same side where possible, and avoid standing too early when the subject prepares to leave. A surveillance-conscious subject may move towards the door and return to a seat to test whether anyone follows.

Pretext involves approaching a person under a limited cover story to create an opportunity for conversation or observation. It carries significant legal and ethical risk. Investigators should use it only when trained, authorised and necessary. They must not impersonate police, government officers, journalists, banks, insurers, medical providers or other real organisations. They must not trick banks, employers, neighbours or family members into disclosing restricted information. Any pretext should minimise deception, avoid entry into homes, avoid unrelated personal matters and focus only on the lawful objective.
## Reports and records
Surveillance reports must be accurate, chronological, objective and complete. The report should identify who conducted the work, the client or matter, the subject, the location, the date, the start and finish times, the weather, the equipment, the vantage point and each relevant observation. The report should distinguish direct observation from inference. It should describe conduct rather than exaggerate conclusions.

Useful reporting includes exact times, movements, physical actions, distances, vehicle descriptions, registration details, people present, photos or video files captured and periods with no activity. Supervisors may add notes about checks, database searches or recommendations, but they should preserve the investigator's observations as a separate record.

Well-planned surveillance can resolve fraud, theft, injury, insurance, workplace and corporate investigations. Poorly planned surveillance can breach privacy, endanger staff, damage evidence and increase liability. The best practice is lawful, targeted, proportionate, well documented and subject to continuous supervision.
