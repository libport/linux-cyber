# Chapter 5: Collection of Information
## Core rules
Information is the foundation of investigative work. Evidence may be documentary, testimonial, digital or physical. A conclusion about a person's character, conduct, ownership or reliability needs corroboration, not rumour. Investigators should collect information that is relevant, accurate, current, complete and capable of verification.

Information gathering also requires legal restraint. Privacy, consumer reporting, financial records, government records, criminal history information and electronic transfers are regulated in the United States. Access does not make use lawful. Investigators should identify the jurisdiction, purpose, authority, consent requirement, retention rule and disclosure limit before collecting personal information. They should preserve audit trails, record sources and avoid back channels that depend on personal favours from police or public officials.
## Collection planning
Investigators should start with a clear information plan. The plan should identify the allegation or question, the facts that need proof, the records that may prove or disprove those facts and the lawful route for obtaining each record. A plan reduces fishing expeditions and helps investigators justify why sensitive material was collected.

Verification should use triangulation. A database hit may point to a person, address, business or asset, but a single hit rarely proves the matter on its own. Investigators should compare independent sources, check dates, resolve name variations and preserve negative results when they explain why a line of inquiry stopped.
## Contacts and leads
Effective investigators build lawful contact networks. Useful sources may include court staff, licensing agencies, property offices, insurance staff, business registries, libraries, professional associations, repair businesses, utility providers and community members. Contacts supply leads, context and directions to records, but they do not replace lawful access procedures. Every lead still needs testing against independent evidence.

A practical source index improves speed and consistency. The index should map each information need to a lawful source, such as property ownership to land records, company details to corporation registries, motor vehicle matters to transport authorities, litigation to court records and professional status to licensing bodies. Investigators should update the index as agencies, databases and online portals change.
## Public records
Public records can provide court filings, property documents, vital records, licensing records, business registrations, tax liens, voter information and motor vehicle records. Access varies by jurisdiction and record type. Some records are open, some are partially redacted, some require consent or proof of purpose and some are restricted to authorised agencies.

The social security number example shows the danger of relying on outdated investigative lore. Before 25 June 2011, the first three digits often reflected an area allocation. Since randomisation began, those digits no longer indicate a state or residence. Investigators should treat older number tables as historical aids only.

Federal, state and local agency lists also need current checking. The Veterans Administration is now the Department of Veterans Affairs. The Civil Aeronautics Board no longer operates. Aviation and transport inquiries may involve the Federal Aviation Administration, Department of Transportation or National Transportation Safety Board, depending on the issue. Banking regulation may involve the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency, not a controller. Firearms and explosives inquiries use the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives. Current names matter because requests sent to the wrong body waste time and may compromise accuracy.
## Private sources, databases and open-source research
Private information services, legal databases, business databases, directories, newspapers, libraries and research services can locate people, assets, businesses, licences, court records, media history and industry context. These tools can accelerate research, but they can also contain stale, mismatched or unlawfully obtained data. Investigators should verify important results through primary records and document the date, source and search terms used.

Libraries remain valuable because they preserve directories, newspapers, law reports, technical material, historical collections and subscription databases. Newspapers and archives can reveal obituaries, business activity, reputational information, addresses, photographs, litigation background and community context.

Internet and social media research can expose public statements, associations, movements, employment claims, photographs and patterns of behaviour. Investigators should use current platform names and preserve context. X, formerly Twitter, Facebook, Instagram, YouTube, LinkedIn, TikTok, Threads, Reddit and messaging ecosystems may all hold relevant material. Screenshots should record URLs, dates, visible metadata and the method used to capture the material. Private messages, locked accounts and deception-based access raise legal and ethical risks.
## Background investigations
Background investigations test whether a person is suitable, qualified and reliable for a role, transaction or legal matter. A sound background investigation may examine identity, employment history, education, licences, criminal history where lawful, motor vehicle records, reputation, financial conduct, property interests and conflicts of interest. Employment checks require particular care because credit reports, consumer reports, criminal history information and adverse decisions often trigger notice, consent and fairness requirements.

Investigators should define the purpose, obtain required authorities, use standardised forms, search the relevant jurisdictions, verify primary records and record limitations. They should distinguish convictions from arrests, allegations from findings and verified facts from reputation. Reports should state the scope, sources, results, gaps and conclusions without overstating what the records prove.
## Real property and asset research
Real property records provide durable evidence about ownership, residence, financial obligations and asset movements. Title searches can reveal grantors, grantees, mortgages, liens, judgments, tax debts, trusts, building permits, related parties, prior addresses, transfers and satisfaction of obligations. Investigators may search by seller, buyer, parcel number, street address, deed book, deed number, plan or transaction date.

When a basic title search stalls, investigators should review related records, including tax records, Uniform Commercial Code filings, mortgage books, judgment indexes and secured notes. Asset findings should be checked against current land records because property data can lag behind transfers, court orders or registry updates.
## Evidence quality and case management
Information becomes useful when investigators organise it and test it. Each item should be linked to its source, collection date, relevance and reliability. Records should support, refute or redirect the working theory. Good case management prevents duplication, protects sensitive material and allows another competent person to follow the reasoning.

Investigators should collect only what the assignment requires, keep personal information secure, correct known errors, avoid unnecessary disclosure and retain records according to law and policy. Effective information gathering combines curiosity with restraint. It uses lawful access, accurate records, disciplined verification and clear reporting to produce findings that can withstand review.
