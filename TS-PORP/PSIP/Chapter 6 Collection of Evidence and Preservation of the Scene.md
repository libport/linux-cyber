# Chapter 6: Collection of Evidence and Preservation of the Scene
## Core duty
Evidence has value only when investigators can show where it came from, how it was protected, who handled it and whether its condition remained reliable. Private security investigators therefore need disciplined scene control, lawful access, careful documentation and a complete chain of custody. The same core standards apply to criminal allegations, civil injuries, insurance claims, arson inquiries, vehicle collisions, workplace violence, theft, fraud, bomb threats, digital incidents and other security matters.

The investigator's first obligation is to protect life and safety. Evidence preservation comes next, but it must not obstruct police, emergency responders or lawful public authority. Private personnel should identify themselves, state their role, keep clear of official operations and avoid entering or searching a scene unless they have a lawful basis and an operational need.

A prepared evidence kit supports careful work. Essential items include personal protective equipment, barrier tape, evidence labels, seals, paper bags, rigid containers, sterile swabs, biohazard bags, trace evidence packets, lighting, measuring tools, markers, sketch materials, a camera, a scale, a tripod, a latent print kit and clean collection tools. Specialist kits may be needed for blood, fingerprints, footwear and tyre impressions, trace materials, biological evidence, firearms residue, excavation, documents and digital evidence. The investigator should use only tools they are trained to use. When specialised evidence is involved, the investigator should secure the scene and call an appropriately qualified expert.
## Initial scene response
The first response sets the quality of the whole investigation. The investigator should record the time, location, report source, nature of the incident, people present, vehicles nearby, weather, lighting, odours, sounds and any visible hazards. They should also note people or vehicles leaving the area, possible entry and exit routes and any signs that evidence has already been moved.

The investigator should treat the location as an evidentiary scene until a competent assessment shows otherwise. Scenes are fragile. Weather, crowds, emergency action, light, heat, animals, cleaning, machinery and ordinary movement can destroy evidence quickly. The first perimeter should be wider than the apparent focal point because it is usually easier to reduce a perimeter than expand it after contamination has occurred.

Scene control requires practical actions:
- Protect life and call emergency services when required.
- Identify hazards before entering, including weapons, chemicals, biological risks, structural damage, electricity, fire, suspicious packages and hostile persons.
- Set a perimeter with tape, barriers, vehicles, locked doors or assigned personnel.
- Keep unauthorised and non-essential people outside the scene.
- Separate suspects, witnesses and bystanders when lawful and practicable.
- Record every person who enters or leaves the scene.
- Protect transient evidence such as footprints, tyre marks, blood, residue, odours, digital device states and weather-sensitive materials.
- Document any victim, object or item that emergency responders moved.
- Preserve CCTV, access logs, radio traffic, alarm records and other time-sensitive records.
## Search and documentation sequence
A competent search follows a structured sequence. Preparation comes first. The investigator should clarify legal authority, decide who is in charge, identify safety needs, allocate tasks, arrange communications and obtain specialist assistance if the matter is complex.

The preliminary survey organises the search. The investigator conducts a careful walk-through, sets or expands boundaries, identifies evidence possibilities, protects fragile material and develops a working theory. The theory should remain provisional. Evidence may show that the first explanation is wrong.

The investigator should describe the scene from general observations to specific details. The narrative should record what is present, what is missing and what appears out of place. The investigator should avoid collecting evidence during this stage unless immediate loss is likely.

A detailed search then proceeds from visible, accessible evidence to hidden or less obvious areas. Suitable search patterns include strip, grid, spiral and zone searches. The selected method should fit the location, number of searchers and evidence type. Investigators should photograph, sketch, label and log each item before collection. Each item should receive an evidence number that matches the notes, photographs, sketch and packaging.

The final survey checks completeness. The investigator should confirm that all evidence is accounted for, packaging is sealed, labels are accurate, notes and logs are complete, equipment has been collected and hard-to-access areas have not been missed. The scene should be released only after that review. The record should state when the scene was released, who released it and to whom it was released. Further entry may require fresh consent, a warrant or another lawful basis.
## Scene protection and specialist risks
Every person at the scene can alter evidence. Investigators must control movement, reduce unnecessary handling and use personal protective equipment. Gloves, masks, eye protection and protective clothing may protect both evidence and personnel. Gloves should be changed when moving between items or locations to avoid cross-contamination.

Explosives, suspected improvised devices and hazardous materials require particular restraint. Clues can include disturbed ground, unusual wires, tape, string, fishing line, batteries, switches, new plaster, loose fittings, abandoned containers, unusual odours, recent digging, out-of-place objects or signs of concealment. Absence of clues does not mean safety. Private personnel should not touch, move, open or photograph a suspicious item at close range. They should withdraw, isolate the area, prevent radio or phone use where instructed by specialists and contact police or bomb response personnel.

Death scenes require accurate observation without amateur interpretation. Post-mortem changes should be recorded with care. Algor mortis means cooling of the body after death. Livor mortis means gravitational pooling and discolouration of blood. Rigor mortis means stiffening of muscles. These signs can assist a death investigation, but they are affected by environment, body condition, clothing, movement and time. They should not be treated as precise clocks.

Gunshot residue collection should follow the laboratory's current protocol. The outdated paraffin casting test should not be used as a reliable modern method. Collection commonly requires approved adhesive stubs or a lab-supplied kit, prompt sampling and careful packaging to prevent loss or contamination.
## Sketches and measurements
A scene sketch supplements notes and photographs. It records where evidence was found, how items relate to fixed objects and what measurements support the reconstruction. A useful sketch includes the date, time, case identifier, preparer, location, north arrow, scale or scale disclaimer, weather, lighting, legend, evidence numbers and measurements.

Sketching methods include triangulation, baseline or coordinate measurement, cross-projection and a rough sketch followed by a finished version. Accuracy matters more than artistic quality. The investigator should measure from fixed points such as walls, corners, door frames, window frames, fixtures, building corners and numbered poles. They should not estimate distance by pacing. A second person should verify measurements where possible. Corrections should be made at the scene, not guessed later.

For a body, serious injury or object of special significance, measurements should be taken from more than one reference point. The location of the head and feet, or opposite ends of a large item, should be recorded separately. Injury diagrams and body charts may also help in workers compensation, assault, personal injury and disability matters.
## Photography
Photographs preserve a scene that will soon change. They should show the scene fairly, accurately and without distortion. The photographer should begin with overall images, then medium-range images, then close-ups. A scale should appear in close-up evidence photographs when size matters. A matching image without the scale should also be taken to show the item undisturbed.

A photographic log should record the image number, date, time, photographer, location, subject, direction, camera details and any scale or flash use. Digital images should be preserved as originals. Investigators should avoid deleting, editing or compressing originals. Where forensic comparison is likely, higher-quality formats and verified master copies are preferable. Working copies may be used for viewing and reporting.

Useful photographic coverage includes:
- Approaches to the scene.
- Surrounding areas.
- Entry and exit points.
- Overall views from several directions.
- Medium views showing evidence relationships.
- Close-ups of individual items.
- Images before and after evidence removal.
- Images of lighting, obstructions, damage and environmental conditions.
- Images that corroborate or test witness accounts.

Vehicle incidents require prompt photography because traffic control and safety often force early movement of vehicles and debris. Photographs should show the whole scene from each approach, final vehicle positions, damage, points of impact, skid marks, gouges, tyre tracks, debris, road condition, visibility, weather, restraints, vehicle defects and any indicators of alcohol or drug involvement.

Bodily injury photographs should record visible injuries at suitable intervals because bruising and swelling may change over time. Arson and fire scenes require overall fire damage coverage and close attention to possible points of origin. Burglary scenes require exterior and interior views, entry and exit points, damage, containers attacked, tool marks, fingerprints and property disturbance. Possible overdose or substance-related scenes require careful recording of medicines, containers, paraphernalia, residues, notes, body position, resuscitation efforts and drugs administered by medical staff.
## Documentary evidence
Documents often decide the direction of an investigation. Police reports, accident reports, intoxication records, arrest reports, domestic violence reports, radio logs, emergency call records, workplace threat reports, missing person reports, disposition sheets and suspect descriptions may all supply facts, timelines and leads. Investigators should treat reports as evidence to evaluate, not as automatic truth. Early reports may contain observations, hearsay, assumptions and later-corrected information.

A workplace threat report should capture the exact words used, conduct accompanying the threat, the relationship between the parties, witnesses, emotional and physical presentation, prior history, triggers, supervisory response, protective steps and prevention measures. Exact language matters because vague summaries can weaken risk assessment and later review.

Suspect descriptions should focus on observable and specific details. Useful details include height, build, clothing, footwear, voice, language used, accent if relevant, gait, visible scars, tattoos, marks, jewellery, carried items, vehicle details and exact words spoken. Investigators should avoid guessing nationality or using broad racial labels unless the information is lawfully collected, relevant and clearly based on a witness's description.
## Medical, employment and economic records
Medical records are central in personal injury, workers compensation, disability, insurance and related claims. Relevant material may include admission records, ambulance records, emergency department notes, medical imaging, laboratory reports, treatment notes, prescriptions, discharge summaries, bills and rehabilitation records. Economic loss inquiries may require employment records, wage records, payroll records, overtime records, leave records, tax records, benefit records and capacity-for-work documents.

Access to these records requires a valid legal basis. A sound authority should identify the person, the records sought, the recipient, the purpose, the date, the signature and any expiry or revocation mechanism. Broad releases that never expire create privacy and proportionality risks. Health information is sensitive and must be collected, stored, used and disclosed under the applicable privacy, health records, employment and court rules. HIPAA is relevant to many United States health information matters, but it does not replace local law in other jurisdictions.
## Digital evidence and information security
Digital evidence is fragile because ordinary use can alter, overwrite or delete data. Relevant sources include computers, mobile phones, tablets, removable drives, memory cards, smart devices, CCTV systems, access control systems, cloud accounts, messaging platforms, vehicles, printers, routers, backups and logs.

Private investigators should preserve rather than explore digital evidence unless trained and authorised. They should record the device location, screen state, power state, time, network connections, peripherals, visible notifications and surrounding items. They should avoid browsing files, opening messages or changing settings without a forensic plan. Where isolation is required, trained personnel may use approved methods such as Faraday protection, network disconnection or controlled power handling. Each option can carry risks, so the method should match the device and the advice of a digital forensic specialist.

Information security controls protect the evidentiary value of records. Investigators should limit access to authorised personnel, use unique accounts, secure passwords, encryption, controlled storage, audit logs, backups, malware protection and secure disposal. Confidential material should not be stored on uncontrolled media or sent through insecure channels.
## Chain of custody and packaging
Chain of custody records the identification, possession, transfer, storage and handling of evidence from collection to presentation in court or another decision-making process. A reliable chain shows who collected the item, where it was found, when it was collected, how it was packaged, how it was sealed, where it was stored, who received it and when each transfer occurred.

Packaging must prevent loss, contamination and alteration. Small fragments should go into containers that cannot leak. Wet biological evidence should usually be dried under controlled conditions before packaging. Biological items should not be sealed wet in plastic unless a specialist protocol requires it. Known and questioned samples should be packaged separately. Victim, suspect and scene items should never be mixed in the same container. Clothing should be packaged item by item. Firearms should be made safe only by competent personnel, then secured in rigid packaging. Documents should be protected from folding, marking, moisture and indented writing. Sharp items should be secured against injury and contamination.

The investigator should label each container with the case identifier, item number, description, date, time, location, collector and hazard warnings. Seals should cross the opening of the package, and the collector should initial or otherwise authenticate the seal according to policy. Tamper-evident bags, evidence tags and transfer logs help preserve the record.

Impression evidence needs special care. Footwear and tyre impressions should be photographed with a scale on the same plane as the impression. Three-dimensional impressions may need casting with suitable dental stone. General scene photographs are usually inadequate for forensic comparison of impressions because they lack sufficient detail.

The investigator should defer to specialists when evidence requires laboratory knowledge, unusual packaging, hazardous handling or advanced interpretation. An investigation can be competent in every other respect and still fail if evidence is contaminated, lost, mislabelled, over-handled or poorly documented.
## Professional conclusion
Evidence preservation is a disciplined practice. It combines lawful authority, safety, scene control, notes, sketches, photographs, documents, packaging, privacy compliance, specialist judgement and chain of custody. Private investigators and security personnel strengthen their work when they act methodically, avoid unnecessary handling, respect police primacy, preserve fragile evidence and document every material decision. A well-preserved scene protects the integrity of the investigation and the fairness of any later adjudication.
