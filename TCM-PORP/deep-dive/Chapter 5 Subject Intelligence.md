# Chapter 5: Subject Intelligence
## Scope and ethics
Subject intelligence collects, correlates and assesses open-source information about a specific person. It supports investigations, due diligence, threat analysis, safety planning and reporting. The work can reveal how a person interacts with others, conducts business, manages assets, moves between places and uses technology.

A digital society creates traces across websites, devices, public records, commercial data sets, social platforms and leaked databases. With the global population above 8 billion and billions of people using email, even ordinary activity can produce a detailed record. Names, addresses, phone numbers, employment, relatives, friends, identifiers, hobbies, travel, vehicle records, memberships and routine locations can all become useful selectors.

Subject intelligence must remain lawful, necessary and proportionate. Analysts should use open sources, avoid logging into a subject's accounts, document uncertainty, protect sensitive data and obtain stakeholder approval before collecting or storing personal information. Privacy law differs by jurisdiction. European Union privacy rules, national data protection statutes, election laws, court access rules and platform terms can all affect collection, use, storage and disclosure.

Subject intelligence can protect people or harm them. Domestic violence safety work may assess whether a survivor's photos, job details, children's posts or public profiles reveal a location. The same methods can enable doxxing, stalking, harassment and coercion. Ethical analysis asks what harm could follow from collection and how the same information could be misused.
## Digital footprints and pattern of life
A digital footprint is the trail left by internet use, connected devices and online services. It may include account names, email addresses, photographs, reviews, timestamps, device metadata, public posts, domain registrations, breach records and location-linked activity.

Pattern of life analysis uses those traces to understand habits, routines and deviations. It can show where a subject regularly appears, when accounts are active, who interacts with the subject and which events interrupt normal behaviour. Strong pattern analysis uses multiple sources rather than one weak indicator.

Useful questions include:
- What is already known?
- What does the known information suggest?
- What remains unknown?
- Which lawful source can answer the gap?
- Does the timing, location or behaviour conflict with another source?
- Could an outside event explain a delay or change?

Timestamps from posts, reviews, photos and activity logs can reveal working hours, sleep windows, travel patterns and account control. Fitness and location-sharing services can expose routes, entrance points and sensitive sites if users publish activities. Investigators should treat inferred locations and routines as hypotheses until corroborated.

Visualisation helps make pattern analysis usable. Timelines, network charts, maps and pivot diagrams can show relationships that prose hides. These products should distinguish confirmed facts from assumptions and should not invite overconfidence.
## Starting from a name
Names are common starting selectors, but they are often ambiguous. Effective name research accounts for culture, language, transliteration, nicknames, married names, professional names and ordering conventions.

Arabic names may use components such as an ism, nasab, laqab, nisba and kunya, although practice varies by country, family and context. Chinese names usually place the family name before the given name, while romanisation can produce multiple spellings. Russian names commonly include a given name, patronymic and surname. The patronymic comes from the father's given name and often takes a gendered form, such as -ovich or -ovna.

Name searches should test variations rather than assume one format. Analysts can search first-last order, last-first order, initials, nicknames, transliterations, professional names and likely email or username forms. Results require verification through independent sources such as photographs, locations, employment, relatives, known accounts or official records.
## Starting from a username
Usernames act as persistent online identifiers. People often reuse the same handle, or a close variation, across forums, social media, gaming sites, code repositories, marketplaces and comment platforms. A single handle can lead to accounts, profile images, posts, contacts, hobbies, political affiliations, employment details, email addresses, domain names and leaked credentials.

A username can also carry useful clues. Embedded names, initials, dates, places, slogans, hobbies and group references may suggest identity or context. These clues remain assumptions until verified. A date-like number may be a birthday, anniversary or random value. A place abbreviation may refer to a state, country, school or unrelated phrase.

Good username research avoids one-source dependence. Search engines may miss accounts or rank irrelevant results. Dedicated username tools can test a handle across many sites, but results still need manual checking. Analysts should test plausible variations created by removed punctuation, added numbers, reversed word order, shortened names and platform-imposed changes.

Anonymous communities show both the value and danger of username analysis. QAnon began with anonymous posts on 4chan in 2017 and grew through posts known as drops. Later research and reporting suggest multiple likely authors rather than a single conclusively identified person. That example shows why attribution from usernames, writing style and platform activity must remain cautious.
## Starting from an email address
Email addresses reveal identity and account links because they function both as contact points and as account usernames. The local part of an address may expose names, initials, dates, relationships or interests. A business email can reveal an employer and a naming convention for other employees.

Email analysis usually begins with search engines and then moves to specialised tools. Analysts can test the email, the local part, the domain and variants. The same local part may appear as a username elsewhere. Correlation improves when several independent sources connect the same email, profile image, name or account.

Google accounts can create additional pivots when public data is exposed through profile photos, reviews, maps, calendars or other services. Public reviews may indicate travel, leisure habits, work locations or a residential area. Such inferences must be handled carefully because shared accounts, old data and account compromise can mislead analysis.

Domain research can connect an email address to websites, businesses or infrastructure. WHOIS and Registration Data Directory Services can provide registration dates, registrars, name servers and abuse contacts. Personal registrant data is often redacted because of privacy rules, registrar policy or proxy services, so analysts should not assume that a domain owner's name, address, phone number or email will be public. RDAP is increasingly important because it provides structured registration data.

Email verification strengthens every downstream conclusion. A single enumeration result is not enough. A stronger correlation may combine a matching handle, profile photograph, subject name, domain record, public post and breach record.
## Privacy email and breach data
Privacy-focused email providers can reduce exposure, but they do not remove all risk. Messages between users of the same secure provider may be end-to-end encrypted, while messages to external providers often rely on transport encryption unless the sender uses a password-protected or PGP workflow. Metadata such as subject lines and sender or recipient fields may not receive the same protection as message bodies and attachments.

Data breaches can expose email addresses, usernames, passwords, password hints, IP addresses and other personal information. Breach data can answer legitimate questions, such as whether a corporate domain appears in public breach indexes or whether a subject's email has appeared in known breaches. Services such as Have I Been Pwned help users check exposure without handing over raw breach dumps.

Working directly with breach data creates legal, ethical and security risk. Analysts should not use exposed passwords to access accounts. They should avoid unnecessary downloading, storing or redistributing breached personal data. Password reuse and unique password patterns can reveal additional accounts, but using those findings requires explicit authority and careful handling.
## Starting from a phone number
Phone numbers can connect to names, addresses, relatives, employers, social media accounts, messaging apps, property records and public databases. They are weaker identifiers than usernames or email addresses because people change numbers, families share landlines, employers allocate numbers and carriers recycle numbers.

Phone number research should combine search engines, public records, people-search databases, messaging platform checks and associated-person searches. A number linked to a spouse, housemate or employer can corroborate a subject when direct evidence is sparse.

Caller ID spoofing makes phone data especially fragile. A displayed number may not identify the real caller. Scam reports, complaint databases and forum comments may show that a number has been spoofed or used repeatedly in fraud attempts, but those reports require careful interpretation.
## Public records and personal disclosures
Public records are government-created or government-held documents available for public inspection. They can include court records, business filings, property records, judgments, bankruptcy records, tax liens, marriage and divorce records, death records and voter registration information. Access rules vary widely by country, state, territory and local authority.

Public records are valuable because they can verify identity, location, assets, legal history, corporate control and relationships. Federal court records in the United States are available through PACER, which charges per page but waives fees for low quarterly use. Freedom of Information Act requests can obtain federal agency records, although exemptions, redactions and processing delays can limit their value.

State and local records can be richer than federal sources. State court portals, secretary of state business registries, voter registration rules, municipal records and county property systems can provide names, addresses, officers, registered agents, case details and property values. Voter data requires special caution. Some states restrict access or use, and voter registration records do not reveal how a person voted.

Property records can reveal ownership, sale history, value, parcel details and sometimes interior or exterior information through secondary real estate platforms. Short-term rental listings and real estate sites can expose layouts, photographs and access points, but they are secondary sources and may be outdated or inaccurate.

Personal disclosure occurs when a person exposes a sensitive document or detail, such as a passport image, identity card, driver's licence, boarding pass or tax document. Public availability does not remove the duty to minimise harm. Analysts should redact unnecessary sensitive data and report only what answers the intelligence requirement.

International public record work demands legal review. A practice that is lawful in one country may breach privacy, data protection or state secrecy rules in another. Analysts should check the laws where they work and the laws governing the subject, then obtain legal approval before collecting, retaining or publishing personal data.
## Verification and reporting
Subject intelligence should build from selectors to corroborated findings. Names, usernames, emails, phone numbers, domains, public records and breach references become useful only when connected through verifiable evidence. Strong reporting separates fact, inference and speculation. It records the source, date, method, confidence and limitations of each finding.

The strongest reports also consider adversarial misuse. A finding that helps a stakeholder may also expose a person to harassment, fraud or violence. Ethical subject intelligence answers the requirement while collecting the least sensitive data necessary and preserving enough context for responsible decisions.
