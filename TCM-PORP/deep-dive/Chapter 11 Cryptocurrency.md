# Cryptocurrency
Cryptocurrency is a digital representation of value that people can transfer or use for payment. Major systems usually run on decentralised networks rather than central banks, although centralised and issuer-controlled digital assets also exist. Market demand, not legislated tender status, normally sets value. Legitimate uses include payments, investment, transfers, and decentralised applications. The same features that reduce reliance on intermediaries - speed, borderless movement, pseudonymity, and irreversible settlement - also expose users to fraud, sanctions evasion, trafficking, money laundering, and terrorism financing.
## Fraud and open-source intelligence
OneCoin showed that criminals can exploit crypto language without operating a genuine blockchain. From 2014 to 2019, Ruja Ignatova, Karl Sebastian Greenwood, and others sold a fraudulent token through multi-level marketing and caused more than USD 4 billion in losses. Analysts can combine subject intelligence, business records, financial intelligence, and blockchain data to map promoters, shell entities, victim flows, public claims, and assets.
## Wallets and transactions
Cryptocurrency relies on cryptographic hashes and digital signatures to prove ownership and authorise transactions. A wallet does not hold coins. It stores or controls private keys that unlock assets recorded on a blockchain. Public addresses receive funds. Private keys or seed phrases authorise spending and must remain secret. Lost keys can make assets unrecoverable.

Most public blockchains preserve visible transaction records. Analysts can usually see addresses, amounts, times, transaction hashes, and movement between wallets, but not names. Attribution usually requires off-chain pivots, including exchange records, sanctions data, usernames, social media, business filings, seized infrastructure, or address reuse.
## Blockchain and validation
A blockchain groups verified transactions into blocks and links each block to earlier data through cryptographic hashes. Later records expose unauthorised alteration. Networks use consensus mechanisms to agree on valid transactions and reduce double-spending risk. Bitcoin uses proof-of-work mining. Ethereum uses proof-of-stake. Solana uses proof-of-history timing alongside proof-of-stake.

Mining creates new coins and validates blocks on proof-of-work systems. Validators or stakers perform related functions on proof-of-stake systems. Mining pools combine computing power and divide rewards. Bridges that move assets between chains create major attack surfaces. In 2022, Wormhole attackers minted 120,000 wrapped ether without equivalent collateral.
## Privacy, mixers, and regulation
Most cryptocurrencies are pseudonymous rather than anonymous. Monero obscures senders, recipients, and amounts. Mixers and tumblers blend transactions to hide origin and destination. These tools can protect legitimate privacy, but criminals also use them to launder stolen funds. OFAC sanctioned Tornado Cash in 2022, then delisted it in 2025 after legal and policy challenges.
## Assets and identifiers
- Bitcoin uses BTC, was proposed in 2008, and has a supply cap of about 21 million coins.
- Ether uses ETH on Ethereum, which supports smart contracts and has dynamic supply because issuance and fee burning change over time.
- BNB now centres on BNB Smart Chain, where addresses use 0x formatting. Legacy Beacon Chain addresses used bnb.
- Tether is a stablecoin issued across multiple networks, so address format depends on the chain.
- Solana addresses are base58-encoded 32-byte values, not a fixed prefix.
- Dogecoin has no maximum supply cap.
- Monero raw addresses are long and start with 4 or 8.
## Analysis priorities
Cryptocurrency analysis should start with an observable fact: an address, transaction hash, username, scam domain, wallet screenshot, exchange account, receipt, or marketplace listing. Analysts then build links through blockchain explorers, sanctions and wanted-person databases, exchange references, open business records, social media, dark web artefacts, forum posts, smart-contract activity, bridge use, and mixer exposure.

A wallet may indicate control, exposure, or contact with illicit funds, but it does not prove identity without corroboration.
