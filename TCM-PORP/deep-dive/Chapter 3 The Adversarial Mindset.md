# Chapter 3: The Adversarial Mindset
Open-source intelligence (OSINT) analysts improve reporting when they assess a target as an adversary would. Attackers look for visible assets, exposed data, weak authentication, reusable credentials, public staff information, and paths that reduce effort. Analysts should examine an organisation or person from the outside, identify the information an attacker can see, and explain why that information has value.

Common attack methods show how public information becomes useful. A brute-force password attack tries many combinations until one works. Credential stuffing tests stolen username and password pairs at other services, which succeeds when users reuse credentials. Phishing uses deceptive messages or websites to obtain sensitive data. Spear phishing targets a specific person, while whaling targets senior or high-value people. Typosquatting registers or uses look-alike domains that exploit typing errors. URL redirection can send users from a trusted-looking link to a malicious site.

OSINT can support analysis by finding weak password patterns, exposed credential pairs, high-value staff, impersonation domains, DNS records, IP addresses, Whois data, and historical website captures. This information helps stakeholders understand practical exposure and prioritise remediation.

An adversary profile should capture means, motives, opportunities, likely tools, active periods, resources, priorities, and detectable patterns. The burglar view of a house illustrates the same logic: attackers compare access points, visibility, cover, perimeter security, likely reward, and likely consequences. Cyber attackers make a similar risk-reward calculation. If a more attractive target suddenly appears, such as a leaked data store or misconfigured system, they may abandon a harder plan and take the easy path.

Analysts should think like attackers without acting like attackers. Passive reconnaissance uses public sources and previously indexed data without logging in, scanning a target, interacting with users, or testing accounts. Active reconnaissance includes direct interaction with systems or people, such as network scanning or unauthorised account access. Exposed credentials may demonstrate data leakage, but analysts must not use them to access accounts.

Tools such as Shodan complicate the boundary. Shodan gathers public information by querying internet-connected devices and presents those results to users. Using such a service avoids direct scanning by the analyst, but it does not make activity inherently unattributable because queries, accounts, and network access may still be logged. Effective OSINT therefore combines adversarial thinking with legal authority, restraint, and operational security.
