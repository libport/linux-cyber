# Introduction to OSINT
Open-source intelligence (OSINT) is intelligence produced from publicly available information. Cybersecurity teams, law enforcement agencies, businesses, and attackers use it to understand people, organisations, assets, and risks without directly accessing private systems.

Public sources can reveal:
- employee names, roles, contact details, and team structures
- technologies mentioned in job advertisements
- geotags and device metadata in social media images
- personal details that support profiling, hiring checks, or social engineering
- weak operational security that may help identify cybercriminals

Defenders use OSINT to run public exposure assessments. They review what employees, systems, domains, old portals, and internet-facing assets reveal online, then reduce risk through removal, hardening, awareness training, and social media policies. This reduces the attack surface, which is the set of entry points an adversary could use.

Law enforcement agencies may use OSINT to support investigations, locate persons of interest, identify poorly concealed online personas, and assist missing-person enquiries. Businesses use OSINT to monitor competitors, market activity, customer behaviour, leaked credentials, confidential disclosures, and signs of planned attacks.

Attackers use the same public information for target reconnaissance, exploit selection, social engineering, and spear phishing. Offensive security teams use comparable methods during authorised assessments to tailor tests and reduce unnecessary noise.

Common OSINT roles include:
- threat intelligence analyst
- security analyst
- vulnerability analyst
- penetration tester
- red team operator
